Elements in a genuinely effective ABMS objective

The Problem with Simple Objectives

When organisations implement an Anti-Bribery Management System under ISO 37001, one of the most important — and most frequently mishandled — requirements is the setting of objectives.

Ask most compliance teams what their ABMS objectives look like and you will hear something like: 'Complete mandatory training for all employees.' Or: 'Conduct the annual risk assessment.' Or, with slightly more ambition: 'Ensure 100% of high-risk third parties complete due diligence.'

These are not bad objectives. But they are, in most cases, insufficiently designed. They measure activity. They do not measure effectiveness. They track whether something was done, not whether doing it made any difference. And in a world where regulators, enforcement bodies, and certifiers are increasingly sophisticated about what genuine compliance looks like versus what paper compliance looks like, the difference matters enormously.

ISO 37001:2025 is explicit on this point. Clause 6.2 requires that ABMS objectives be measurable, take into account applicable requirements, be relevant to identified bribery risks, and be monitored and updated as appropriate. The Standard does not say objectives should be easy to achieve. It says they should be meaningful — genuinely connected to the risks the organisation faces and capable of demonstrating whether the system is actually working.

This article argues that most organisations are leaving significant value on the table when they design their ABMS objectives — and that the organisations that get this right are not only better protected against bribery risk, but better positioned to defend their programme when it comes under external scrutiny.

What Makes an ABMS Objective Genuinely Effective?

A well-constructed ABMS objective does five things simultaneously. It is connected to a specific, identified bribery risk in the organisation's risk register — not a generic category, but a named risk with a reference number. It has a measurable target that can be tracked over time and that tells the organisation whether it is improving, static, or going backwards. It explains how the measurement will actually be performed — which system, which data, which process, which person. It has a defined timeframe that creates accountability without being arbitrary. And it is owned by a named individual who is responsible for its achievement.

The SMART framework — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound — is a useful starting point. But the application of SMART to ABMS objectives requires more rigour than it does in, say, a performance review. Because the stakes are different. An ABMS objective that looks SMART on paper but is designed to be easily achievable rather than genuinely informative is not serving its purpose. It is generating false assurance.

The Traps Most Organisations Fall Into

Trap 01

Measuring Completion, Not Comprehension

Training completion rates are the classic example. An organisation can achieve '95% training completion' with a population who clicked through without reading a word. A stronger objective measures the average post-assessment score and sets a minimum pass mark.

Trap 02

Measuring Inputs, Not Outcomes

'Conduct the annual risk assessment' is an input objective — it tells you something happened. A better objective measures whether the risk assessment refreshed the risk profile, identified new risks, and received Top Management approval.

Trap 03

Detachment from the Risk Profile

An objective designed for the organisation as a whole — set at a comfortable global average — may mask serious underperformance in the markets that matter most. Effective ABMS objectives are sometimes global, sometimes regional, sometimes country-specific.

Trap 04

Ignoring the Behavioural Dimension

Compliance programmes exist to change behaviour. But most ABMS objectives measure process compliance, not behavioural change. Employee surveys, culture scores, and behavioural metrics derived from system data are significantly more valuable — and harder to design.

Anatomy of a Well-Constructed ABMS Objective

Before looking at the breadth of what a good objectives programme covers, it is worth examining the structure of a single well-constructed objective in detail. The following is a fully worked example using OBJ-005: reducing the turnaround time for Level 1 due diligence reports.

Field Content (OBJ-005) Why This Field Exists
Relevant Risk Delays in due diligence reports create pressure to proceed before screening is complete Every objective must be anchored to a specific, identified risk — this forces the question of what bribery risk the objective is actually managing
Risk Reference BR-013 — Due Diligence Process Delay and Bypass Risk Creates a traceable link from objective back to the risk register, demonstrating the programme is risk-driven, not activity-driven
Scope Global Defines who is accountable and prevents global averages from masking poor performance in specific markets
Objective Statement Reduce average Level 1 DD turnaround from 15 to 7 business days by Q3 2026 Captures what success looks like — must include a baseline, target, and timeframe. 'Improve turnaround' is not an objective statement
How We Measure It KYC system records request submission and report completion dates; monthly average calculated by region and DD type The field most organisations skip. Specifies the system, frequency, granularity, and the response when performance falls short
Target Average ≤ 7 business days by Q3 2026 Specific, numeric, and time-bound — reflects real-world variation while still creating meaningful accountability
Owner Head of Due Diligence / Compliance Manager Named ownership is non-negotiable. An objective without an owner is a wish

The action plan that sits beneath each objective — a numbered set of specific tasks, owners, and due dates — closes the loop between the objective and execution. An objective without an action plan is a statement of intent. An objective with a numbered action plan is a management commitment.

The Architecture Behind Good Objectives

What distinguishes effective objectives from a typical ABMS register is architecture. Each one is built on three foundations that most organisations underinvest in.

Traceability

Every objective linked to a specific risk register entry by reference number — creating an auditable chain from risk to objective to action to outcome.

Scope Granularity

Not every objective should be global. The most important objectives acknowledge where real risk concentrations are — country-specific or market-specific where needed.

Measurement Discipline

The measurement methodology is not a footnote — it is the objective. If you cannot describe precisely how data will be collected, the objective is not yet finished.

What Auditors and Regulators Are Looking For

The shift from ISO 37001:2016 to ISO 37001:2025 has brought with it a clearer expectation that objectives are substantive, not ceremonial. Certification auditors are increasingly probing not just whether objectives exist, but whether they are genuine indicators of programme performance. Can the organisation demonstrate trend data over time? Can it explain what happened when a target was missed? Are objectives reviewed and updated when the risk landscape changes?

Regulatory enforcement bodies apply a similar lens. The US Department of Justice, the UK Serious Fraud Office, and equivalent agencies have consistently indicated in published guidance that credit is given for programmes that are demonstrably effective — not merely formally implemented.

An objectives register that shows the organisation measuring the things that matter is significantly more valuable in a mitigation argument than one that records a series of easily completed process activities.

There is also a board governance dimension. Telling a board that anti-bribery training completion was 94% tells them almost nothing about whether the organisation is protected. Telling them that investigation closure times dropped from 94 days to 58 days, that a culture score in a high-risk market improved by 20 percentage points, and that the error rate in due diligence reports fell from 22% to 6% — that tells them something they can act on.

Getting Started

Redesigning an ABMS objectives register is not a one-day exercise, but it does not need to be a transformation project either. The following steps provide a practical path for organisations looking to move from activity-based to outcome-based objectives.

Conclusion

ISO 37001:2025 sets a clear expectation: ABMS objectives should be meaningful, measurable, and connected to identified bribery risks. Most organisations meet the minimum threshold. The organisations that genuinely distinguish themselves — in the eyes of certifiers, regulators, boards, and their own employees — are the ones that go further.

They design objectives that measure outcomes, not just activities. They scope objectives at the level of granularity that reflects where risk actually concentrates. They invest in the measurement architecture that makes tracking and accountability possible. And they review and update their objectives as the business and its risk profile evolve.

The twenty-five examples in this article share a common characteristic: they are built to tell an organisation something true and useful about whether its anti-bribery programme is working. In an environment where bribery risk is persistent, regulators are sophisticated, and the consequences of failure are severe — that is exactly what an ABMS objective should do.

Practical Guidance

Getting Started

A practical path for organisations moving from activity-based to outcome-based ABMS objectives.

1

Start with the Risk Register

Every objective should have a parent risk. If an objective cannot be linked to a specific identified risk, it should be questioned.

2

Distinguish Activity vs Outcome

Activity metrics have their place as supporting data. Identify at least one outcome metric for each material risk area.

3

Scope Deliberately

For each objective, ask: is this risk global, regional, or concentrated in specific markets? Design at the right level.

4

Design Measurement First

Work out how data will be collected, from which system, at what frequency. The target number comes after the method is defined.

5

Build in a Review Cycle

Objectives should be reviewed at every management review meeting. The best objectives registers are living documents.

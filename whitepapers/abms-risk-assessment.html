ISO 37001 Clause 4.5 requires organisations to conduct a documented bribery risk assessment. Most organisations produce one. Far fewer produce one that would survive scrutiny by a certification auditor, a regulator, or a court. The difference lies almost entirely in specificity, methodology, and the discipline of separating gross risk from net risk.

This whitepaper sets out the technical requirements of an anti-bribery risk assessment that is genuinely fit for purpose — one that can support ISO 37001 certification, inform control design, and provide real governance value rather than serving as a compliance artefact.

1. The Standards Framework

Three international standards govern the design and conduct of an anti-bribery risk assessment within an Anti-Bribery Management System (ABMS). Each plays a distinct role, and a well-constructed assessment draws on all three.

ISO 37001:2016 — Clause 4.5 is the compliance anchor. It requires the organisation to document a bribery risk assessment that considers the nature, scale, and complexity of activities; the countries and sectors in which the organisation operates; the nature of its business relationships; the transactions, projects, and activities it undertakes; the regulatory and enforcement context; and the conduct of associated persons.

ISO 31000:2018 provides the risk methodology. Its three-stage framework — Risk Identification, Risk Analysis, and Risk Evaluation — supplies the procedural backbone for moving from a list of possible risks through to a scored, prioritised, and treatment-ready risk register. ISO 31000 also establishes the importance of context (both internal and external) before any risk identification begins.

ISO 37000:2021 adds the governance layer. The governing body is accountable for defining and approving the organisation's risk appetite, and that appetite must be articulated before net risk scores can be interpreted against a threshold that triggers escalation or treatment.

Standard Role in Risk Assessment Key Contribution
ISO 31000:2018 Risk methodology framework Identification, Analysis, and Evaluation stages; context-setting; treatment options
ISO 37001:2016 Compliance requirement and scope definition Clause 4.5 mandatory factors; documentation requirements; review triggers; associated persons scope
ISO 37000:2021 Governance accountability Governing body accountability for risk appetite; oversight of assessment process; approval of treatment plans

2. The Specificity Imperative

The single most common failure in anti-bribery risk assessment is operating at the wrong level of abstraction. Organisations conduct assessments at the category level — "procurement bribery risk", "third-party risk", "permit-related risk" — and assign each category a rating. The output is a heat map. The heat map is filed. No useful action follows.

This approach fails for three reasons. First, a category-level risk cannot be controlled — controls attach to specific transactions, roles, and counterparties, not to abstract categories. Second, a category-level risk cannot be tested — there is no scenario against which a control's effectiveness can be evaluated. Third, category-level assessments consistently fail ISO 37001 certification audits, because the auditor cannot trace the risk to a specific scenario, a specific control, and evidence of that control operating.

Listing 'procurement bribery risk: HIGH' is not a risk assessment. It is a risk acknowledgement. The assessment begins where acknowledgement ends — with the specific question: who, to whom, in which transaction, using what mechanism, under what circumstances, and with what consequence if it occurs?

The Bribery Scenario as the Unit of Analysis

The correct unit of analysis is the bribery scenario: a discrete, fact-specific description of a situation in which bribery could occur. Each scenario identifies the role within the organisation (who might pay or receive), the counterparty (who would receive or pay), the specific transaction or interaction (the context in which the bribery would occur), the form of bribe (cash, gift, hospitality, kickback, equity interest), the intended outcome (a licence, contract award, regulatory approval, reduced inspection frequency), and the conditions that make the scenario plausible.

Four lenses for identifying scenarios:

Transaction Mapping

For each significant transaction category — procurement, sales, customs clearance, permit applications, tax assessments, litigation — identify the points at which a bribe could be offered, solicited, or accepted. Map the counterparties involved at each point.

Interaction Mapping

Identify every recurring interaction between organisational personnel (or associated persons) and public officials, regulatory bodies, law enforcement, or commercial counterparties in positions of discretion. Each interaction type is a potential scenario cluster.

Pressure Point Analysis

Consider the commercial and operational pressures within the business — revenue targets, licence dependencies, supply chain bottlenecks, regulatory approvals — and identify where those pressures create incentives for individuals to consider bribery as a solution.

Historical and Intelligence Analysis

Draw on internal incident history, whistleblower reports, due diligence findings, sector enforcement actions (FCPA, Bribery Act, OECD country reports), and corruption perception indices for all relevant markets. Reported cases in similar organisations define plausible scenarios for yours.

Geography Matters: the Same Company, Different Scenarios

A scenario that is implausible in one jurisdiction may be the primary risk in another. The assessment must be jurisdiction-specific. The following illustrates how the same company function — customs clearance — generates different scenarios across three geographies:

Jurisdiction Scenario Risk Driver
Singapore Logistics officer facilitates accelerated clearance through agent network; agent absorbs facilitation payment without disclosure Low direct risk; agent opacity is the concern
Indonesia Local operations manager pays customs official directly to release goods held pending inspection; payment processed as "storage fee" through petty cash Facilitation payment with false accounting; high likelihood in specific ports
France Commercial director offers hospitality package to procurement official of state-owned enterprise during tender evaluation period Commercial kickback / improper advantage; Sapin II and Bribery Act exposure

3. Gross Risk: The Inherent Baseline

Gross risk — sometimes called inherent risk — is the level of exposure that exists before any controls are applied. It reflects the raw vulnerability of a given scenario given the nature of the business, the jurisdiction, the transaction, and the counterparty, assuming nothing has been done to prevent or detect the bribery.

Many organisations skip gross risk assessment entirely, proceeding directly to a net risk score that incorporates the effect of existing controls. This is a significant error. Without a documented gross risk baseline, the control framework becomes invisible: you cannot demonstrate what your controls are contributing, you cannot identify which risks are well-controlled and which are not, and you cannot defend your net risk scores under audit because there is no documented reasoning for the reduction applied.

Gross risk tells you what you are exposed to. Net risk tells you what you have done about it. The difference between them is the contribution of your controls.

Likelihood and Consequence Scales

Gross risk is calculated as the product of likelihood and consequence, each scored on a defined scale. The scales must be calibrated to the organisation's context and applied consistently across all scenarios.

Likelihood scale (1–5):

Score Rating Indicative Description
1 Rare Could occur only in exceptional circumstances; no known precedent in sector or jurisdiction
2 Unlikely Could occur at some time; limited precedent; structural factors make it improbable
3 Possible Might occur; known precedent in sector or jurisdiction; conditions present
4 Likely Will probably occur in most circumstances; precedent exists within comparable organisations
5 Almost Certain Expected to occur; known occurrence within the organisation or close sector peers

Consequence scale (1–5): Consequence should be evaluated across four dimensions — legal/regulatory, financial, reputational, and operational — and the highest dimension score used as the consequence rating for the scenario.

Score Rating Legal/Regulatory Financial
1 Minor Administrative breach; internal remediation only Immaterial financial impact
2 Moderate Regulatory notice; limited enforcement action Up to 1% of annual revenue
3 Significant Formal investigation; potential prosecution of individuals 1–5% of annual revenue
4 Major Corporate prosecution; significant financial penalties 5–15% of annual revenue
5 Catastrophic Debarment, licence loss, criminal conviction of senior personnel Existential financial impact

The risk matrix maps the product of these two scores (1–25) to a rating band. Scores of 20–25 are EXTREME; 15–19 are HIGH; 8–14 are MEDIUM; and 1–7 are LOW. These thresholds must be defined before scoring begins and approved by management.

4. Controls: Mapping, Testing, and Net Risk

Once gross risk scores are established, the assessment turns to the control framework. For each scenario, the assessor identifies every control that is in place that could reduce the likelihood that the bribery occurs, or the consequence if it does. Controls are then tested, and a net risk score is calculated based on the tested effectiveness of those controls.

Types of Controls

Preventive Controls

Designed to reduce the likelihood that a bribery scenario occurs. Examples: third-party due diligence, gifts and hospitality pre-approval, segregation of duties in procurement, prohibition on facilitation payments, mandatory anti-bribery training.

Detective Controls

Designed to identify that a bribery event has occurred or is occurring. Examples: expense report review, whistleblower channel, transaction monitoring, internal audit, management review of anomalous payments.

Corrective Controls

Designed to contain damage and prevent recurrence following a detected event. Examples: investigation protocol, disciplinary procedures, regulatory self-reporting, remediation planning, clawback provisions.

An untested control is a hypothesis. When you record a net risk score based on controls that have not been tested, you are not assessing risk — you are expressing hope.

Control Testing Framework

Each control must be evaluated against three questions before it can be credited in the net risk calculation:

Design Adequacy

Is the control designed to address the risk?

A control that is well-designed but addresses a different risk, or that has no logical connection to the bribery scenario, cannot reduce the net risk for that scenario. Design adequacy is assessed by reviewing the control's documented purpose, scope, and mechanism.

Implementation

Has the control been implemented as designed?

A policy that exists on paper but has never been communicated, trained, or operationalised is not a functioning control. Implementation testing requires evidence that the control exists in the real operating environment — not just in the policy library.

Operating Effectiveness

Is the control operating effectively over time?

Operating effectiveness testing requires evidence of actual performance: approval records, training completion logs, due diligence files, exception reports, audit findings. A control that operated effectively six months ago may not be operating effectively today.

Control Effectiveness Ratings and Net Risk Calculation

Following testing, each control is rated on its effectiveness. These ratings determine how much the gross risk score is reduced:

Effectiveness Rating Definition Score Reduction
Effective Designed adequately, implemented, operating with evidence of consistent performance Reduces likelihood by 2–3 points
Partially Effective Designed adequately but implementation gaps or inconsistent operation noted Reduces likelihood by 1 point
Ineffective Control exists but testing reveals it is not operating as designed No reduction applied
Not In Place No control exists for this scenario No reduction; gap identified for treatment

A critical point: controls primarily reduce likelihood, not consequence. The consequence of a bribery event — prosecution, financial penalty, reputational damage — is determined by the nature of the act, the jurisdiction, and enforcement practice, not by the organisation's internal control framework. Adjusting consequence scores downward based on the existence of controls is a methodological error that inflates optimism and deflates treatment urgency.

Worked example: A Vietnamese sales representative has discretion over distributor selection and pricing. The gross risk score for a commercial kickback scenario is: Likelihood 4 (Likely) x Consequence 5 (Catastrophic, given FCPA and local law exposure) = 20 (EXTREME). After testing, a third-party due diligence control is rated Partially Effective (implementation gaps in annual refresh), and a gifts and hospitality pre-approval policy is rated Effective. The net likelihood is reduced from 4 to 2. Net risk = 2 x 5 = 10 (HIGH). The scenario remains HIGH, requiring senior management attention and a documented treatment plan.

5. Risk Evaluation and Treatment

Risk evaluation is the process of comparing net risk scores against the organisation's defined risk appetite to determine which risks require treatment and at what level of escalation. Risk appetite must be defined by management and approved by the governing body before the evaluation phase begins — not after.

Escalation Thresholds

Net Risk Rating Escalation Level Required Response
EXTREME (20–25) Board / Governing Body Immediate treatment required; board-approved plan; progress reported at each board meeting
HIGH (15–19) Senior Management Treatment plan approved by CEO or equivalent; quarterly progress review; internal audit coverage
MEDIUM (8–14) Management Review Treatment plan at function level; semi-annual review; documented in risk register
LOW (1–7) Standard Monitoring Existing controls maintained; annual review; no additional treatment required unless appetite changes

The Four Treatment Options

Avoid

Withdraw from the activity, market, or relationship that creates the risk. This is appropriate where the gross risk is EXTREME and no combination of controls can reduce it to within appetite. Avoidance must be a deliberate, documented decision — not a default.

Reduce

Implement or strengthen controls to bring the net risk within appetite. The treatment plan must specify which controls will be implemented, by whom, by when, and what evidence will be produced. Generic commitments ("improve due diligence") are not treatment plans.

Share or Transfer

Engage a third party — insurer, joint venture partner, contractual indemnity — to share exposure. Note that legal liability for bribery cannot be transferred: a company cannot indemnify itself against criminal prosecution. Sharing applies primarily to financial consequence, not legal exposure.

Accept

Formally document that the risk falls within appetite and no additional treatment is required. Acceptance is an explicit decision, not the absence of a decision. It must be approved at the appropriate level given the risk rating and reviewed at least annually.

Treatment plans must be specific, actionable, time-bound, and auditable. The risk assessment is not complete until every risk above the organisation's appetite threshold has a documented treatment plan with a named owner and a delivery date.

6. High-Risk Bribery Scenarios: A Reference Library

The following scenario categories represent the most commonly identified high-risk bribery situations across ISO 37001 engagements. Organisations should use these as a starting point for scenario development, adapting each to their specific jurisdictions, business models, and counterparty profiles.

Facilitation Payment — Customs and Border Control

Operations or logistics personnel make payments to customs officials to secure timely release of goods. Payments are coded as storage or handling fees. Relevant in high-volume import/export operations across markets with high corruption perception scores.

Permit Acceleration — Regulatory Authorities

Project or compliance personnel make payments to accelerate the issuance of operating licences, environmental permits, or construction approvals. Particularly prevalent in infrastructure, extractive, and real estate sectors.

Public Procurement — Tender Manipulation

Sales or business development personnel provide financial or other advantages to procurement officials at state-owned enterprises or government bodies to influence tender evaluation. Often structured through intermediaries to obscure the payment chain.

Tax Assessment Negotiation

Finance or tax personnel make payments to tax inspectors to reduce assessed tax liabilities or avoid penalties. Common in jurisdictions where tax enforcement is discretionary and audit outcomes are negotiable.

Judicial and Regulatory Proceedings

Legal or compliance personnel make payments to influence the outcome of regulatory investigations, court proceedings, or enforcement actions. High consequence scenario given the direct involvement of legal institutions.

Commercial Kickbacks — Private Sector

Sales personnel provide undisclosed financial benefits to procurement staff at commercial customers in exchange for contract awards or preferential pricing. The private sector equivalent of public procurement bribery; covered by ISO 37001 and the UK Bribery Act.

Undisclosed Agent or Intermediary

A commercial agent, consultant, or introducer receives a commission that is wholly or partly used to make payments to public officials or commercial counterparties on the organisation's behalf. The organisation fails to conduct adequate due diligence or contractual controls.

Internal: Procurement Staff Receiving Kickbacks

Internal procurement personnel solicit or accept payments from suppliers in exchange for preferential treatment in vendor selection, pricing negotiations, or invoice approval. This is passive bribery — the organisation is the victim as well as the potential perpetrator under inadequate control environments.

Management Conflicts of Interest

Senior personnel approve transactions with entities in which they hold undisclosed financial interests, or influence hiring decisions to benefit family members of business counterparties. Conflicts of interest that create structural opportunities for corruption must be included in the risk assessment scope.

7. Conducting the Risk Assessment: Process and Governance

The risk assessment is not a desk exercise. It requires structured engagement with business units, operational functions, and — where applicable — associated persons. The following phased process reflects the requirements of ISO 37001 Clause 4.5 and the methodology of ISO 31000.

1
Phase 1: Scope Definition
  • Define the organisational scope: legal entities, business units, geographies, and associated persons to be covered
  • Confirm that the scope aligns with the ABMS scope as defined under ISO 37001 Clause 4.3
  • Identify the assessment team, including functional subject matter experts and any external specialist support
  • Obtain approval from the ABMS owner or top management for the scope and methodology before commencing
2
Phase 2: Context Analysis
  • Analyse the external context: jurisdictions, regulatory frameworks, enforcement history, corruption perception data, sector-specific risks
  • Analyse the internal context: business model, revenue streams, transaction types, organisational structure, historical incidents
  • Review existing ABMS documentation: policies, procedures, prior risk assessments, audit findings, due diligence outcomes
  • Confirm or update the organisation's risk appetite statement in consultation with the governing body
3
Phases 3 and 4: Transaction Mapping and Scenario Development
  • Map all significant transaction categories and recurring interactions with public officials and commercial counterparties
  • Conduct structured interviews with business unit leaders, finance, legal, procurement, sales, HR, and operations
  • Develop the full scenario inventory using the four lenses: transaction mapping, interaction mapping, pressure point analysis, and historical and intelligence analysis
  • Document each scenario in sufficient detail to support gross risk scoring and control mapping
  • Validate the scenario inventory with functional leaders before proceeding to scoring
4
Phases 5 and 6: Gross Risk Scoring and Control Mapping
  • Score each scenario for gross likelihood using the defined 1–5 scale with documented rationale
  • Score each scenario for consequence across all four dimensions (legal, financial, reputational, operational)
  • Calculate the gross risk score (likelihood x consequence) and assign the gross risk rating
  • Map every existing control to each scenario it is designed to address
  • Review control documentation: policies, procedures, training records, due diligence files, approval logs
5
Phases 7 and 8: Control Testing and Net Risk Scoring
  • Test each mapped control for design adequacy, implementation, and operating effectiveness
  • Document testing methodology and evidence for each control
  • Assign an effectiveness rating to each control (Effective, Partially Effective, Ineffective, Not In Place)
  • Calculate the net likelihood for each scenario based on tested control effectiveness ratings
  • Calculate the net risk score (net likelihood x consequence) and assign the net risk rating
  • Identify control gaps: scenarios where no effective control is in place to reduce gross likelihood
6
Phases 9 and 10: Risk Evaluation and Treatment Planning
  • Compare each net risk rating against the defined risk appetite thresholds
  • Identify all scenarios where net risk exceeds appetite and requires treatment
  • Develop specific, actionable, time-bound treatment plans for each out-of-appetite scenario
  • Assign treatment plan ownership to named individuals with authority and resources to execute
  • Document acceptance decisions for scenarios that fall within appetite, with appropriate approval
  • Escalate EXTREME and HIGH risks to the appropriate governance level as defined in the escalation framework
7
Phases 11 and 12: Documentation, Approval, Integration, and Monitoring
  • Compile the complete risk assessment report: scope, methodology, scenario inventory, gross and net risk scores, control testing results, treatment plans
  • Obtain formal approval from top management and, where required by scope, the governing body
  • Integrate the risk assessment outputs into ABMS control design: update policies, procedures, and training to address identified gaps
  • Establish the review schedule: at minimum annual, and triggered by significant changes in context
  • Track treatment plan progress through the ABMS monitoring and measurement function (ISO 37001 Clause 9.1)

Review Triggers

ISO 37001 requires the risk assessment to be reviewed and updated when significant changes occur. Triggers that mandate an unscheduled review include: entry into a new market or jurisdiction; acquisition or divestiture of a business; engagement of a new category of associated person; a bribery incident or near-miss within the organisation; a significant enforcement action against a sector peer; material changes to the regulatory or enforcement environment; and significant organisational restructuring affecting control ownership.

8. Common Failures in Anti-Bribery Risk Assessment

The following failures are consistently observed in risk assessments reviewed during ISO 37001 gap analyses, certification pre-audits, and post-incident investigations. Each represents a methodological or governance deficiency that undermines the value and defensibility of the assessment.

Failure Why It Matters Corrective Action
Assessing at category level rather than scenario level Categories cannot be controlled, tested, or used to inform specific treatment actions Rebuild the assessment around discrete, fact-specific bribery scenarios
Applying the same risk profile across all jurisdictions Risk is inherently jurisdiction-specific; a single profile ignores material differences in enforcement, corruption levels, and transaction types Conduct jurisdiction-specific scenario development for each material geography
Conflating gross and net risk Without a gross risk baseline, the control framework's contribution cannot be demonstrated or defended Always score gross risk before applying any control credit
Recording controls without testing them Untested controls may not be operating; net risk scores based on untested controls are unreliable Test every mapped control for design, implementation, and operating effectiveness before applying a credit
Assuming controls reduce consequence Controls primarily reduce likelihood; consequence is determined by the act and the enforcement environment Apply control credit only to likelihood scores; hold consequence constant except in exceptional, documented circumstances
Treating risk acceptance as the absence of action Undocumented acceptance is not governance; it is negligence Require explicit approval documentation for every scenario rated within appetite
Not assessing passive or inbound bribery ISO 37001 covers both active bribery (paying) and passive bribery (receiving); an assessment that covers only outbound bribery is incomplete Include scenarios in which organisational personnel are solicited for bribes or receive undisclosed benefits
Failing to connect risk assessment to control design A risk assessment that does not result in specific control improvements or confirmations has no governance value Require explicit linkage between each gap identified and the control or treatment plan that addresses it
No review following significant events A risk assessment that predates a material change in context may be wholly unreliable as a current picture of exposure Implement and enforce a defined set of review triggers; document when reviews are triggered and what changes result
Generic or aspirational treatment plans "Improve due diligence" is not a treatment plan; it cannot be tracked, tested, or held to account Require treatment plans to specify the control to be implemented, the owner, the deadline, and the evidence of completion

9. What an ISO 37001 Auditor Will Look For

Certification auditors conducting an assessment under ISO 37001 will evaluate the bribery risk assessment across three evidence categories. Understanding the auditor's perspective allows organisations to build an assessment that is both genuinely useful and readily verifiable.

Document Review

The auditor will review the risk assessment document itself, the methodology documentation, the risk appetite statement, individual scenario records, control testing evidence, treatment plans, and approval records. The document must be traceable — every score must have a documented rationale, every control credit must have a corresponding test record, and every treatment plan must have an owner and a date.

Interview Evidence

Auditors will interview the risk assessment owner, functional leaders, and compliance personnel. The interviews test whether the assessment reflects genuine organisational knowledge or was produced as a documentation exercise. Key auditor questions include:

"Walk me through how you identified the bribery scenarios in your Vietnam operations."

The auditor is testing whether the scenario development process was substantive — whether real business knowledge was applied — or whether scenarios were copied from a template without contextual adaptation.

"What is your gross likelihood score for this scenario, and why?"

The auditor expects a reasoned answer based on contextual factors: enforcement precedent, transaction volume, counterparty profile, jurisdictional corruption data. A score without reasoning will not satisfy the auditor.

"How did you test this control, and what did the testing find?"

The auditor expects a specific answer: the testing method (sample review, interview, observation, walkthrough), the sample size, the period covered, and the outcome. "We reviewed the policy and it looked adequate" is not testing.

"Show me the evidence that this control is operating effectively."

For each control credited in the net risk calculation, the auditor will request contemporaneous evidence of operation: approval records, training logs, due diligence reports, exception reports. Evidence that cannot be produced suggests the control credit should not have been applied.

"Has there been any event since your last review that should have triggered a reassessment?"

The auditor is testing whether the review trigger mechanism is understood and actually applied, or whether it exists only in the policy documentation.

Transaction Testing

For specific high-risk scenarios, the auditor may request a sample of underlying transactions to verify that controls are operating as described. This might include a sample of gifts and hospitality approvals, a sample of third-party due diligence files, or a sample of payments to agents in high-risk markets. The findings from transaction testing directly inform the auditor's assessment of whether the net risk scores are supportable.

An auditor's job is not to find a risk assessment document. It is to determine whether the organisation actually knows what its bribery risks are, and whether it has built controls that actually work.

10. Conclusion

The bribery risk assessment is not a compliance artefact to be produced for certification and then filed. It is the engine of the entire ABMS. Every element of ISO 37001 — due diligence scope, control design, training content, monitoring activity, incident response — should derive from the risk assessment. An ABMS that is disconnected from its risk assessment is an ABMS that is managing documentation rather than managing risk.

An assessment that meets the standard requires three disciplines that most organisations find difficult in practice. It requires specificity — the willingness to identify and document discrete, fact-specific scenarios rather than comfortable abstractions. It requires gross-to-net discipline — the commitment to establish an inherent baseline before applying control credits, so that the contribution of the control framework is visible and defensible. And it requires control testing — the rigour to verify that controls are actually working before crediting them in the net risk calculation.

Organisations that invest in building a risk assessment that meets these three disciplines will find that it delivers value far beyond certification: it provides a defensible account of the organisation's bribery exposure, a clear map of where the control framework is strong and where it is weak, and a governance record that demonstrates genuine organisational commitment to anti-bribery compliance.

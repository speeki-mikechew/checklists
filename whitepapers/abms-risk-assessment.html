ISO 37001 Clause 4.5 requires organisations to conduct a documented bribery risk assessment. Most organisations produce one. Far fewer produce one that would survive scrutiny by a certification auditor, a regulator, or a court. The difference lies almost entirely in specificity, methodology, and the discipline of separating gross risk from net risk.

This whitepaper sets out the technical requirements of an anti-bribery risk assessment that is genuinely fit for purpose — one that can support ISO 37001 certification, inform control design, and provide real governance value rather than serving as a compliance artefact.

1. The Standards Framework

Three international standards govern the design and conduct of an anti-bribery risk assessment within an Anti-Bribery Management System (ABMS). Each plays a distinct role, and a well-constructed assessment draws on all three.

1.1 ISO 37001 Clause 4.5 — What Is Actually Required

ISO 37001:2016 — Clause 4.5 is the compliance anchor. It sets out the requirement for anti-bribery risk assessment and is more prescriptive than many organisations recognise. It requires the organisation to identify, analyse, and evaluate bribery risk, taking into account the following factors:

  • The nature, scale, and complexity of the organisation's activities.
  • The countries and sectors in which the organisation operates.
  • The business relationships it has — including business partners, joint ventures, agents, subsidiaries, and supply chain counterparties.
  • The transactions it conducts, particularly those involving government officials, public procurement, or high-value discretionary decisions.
  • The regulatory and legal context in each jurisdiction.
  • The persons associated with the organisation who could commit bribery on its behalf.

These requirements point unmistakably toward specificity. The standard is not asking for a general statement about the organisation's exposure to bribery. It is requiring an analysis of specific risk factors — transactions, relationships, jurisdictions, associated persons — that produce specific risk scenarios which can be scored, controlled, tested, and treated.

Clause 4.5 further requires that the risk assessment be documented, that it be reviewed at planned intervals or when significant changes occur, and that it be proportionate to the nature and scale of the bribery risks the organisation faces. These requirements have direct implications for methodology: a risk assessment that is not documented in a form that can be reviewed, audited, or updated does not satisfy the standard.

1.2 ISO 31000 — The Risk Management Framework

ISO 37001 does not prescribe a specific risk assessment methodology. Organisations are expected to apply recognised risk management principles, and ISO 31000:2018 — Risk Management — provides the appropriate framework.

The ISO 31000 framework identifies three core stages of the risk assessment process: risk identification, risk analysis, and risk evaluation. These are not merely labels — each stage has specific outputs that feed into the next, and shortcutting any stage undermines the integrity of the results.

ISO 31000 Stage What It Means In an ISO 37001 Context
Risk Identification Find and describe all sources of risk, events, and their potential consequences Map every transaction type, function, geography, and counterparty where bribery could occur; develop specific bribery scenarios for each
Risk Analysis Understand the nature of the risk and determine the level of risk through likelihood and consequence assessment Score each identified bribery scenario for gross likelihood and gross consequence; map existing controls; test controls; calculate net risk scores
Risk Evaluation Compare the level of risk against risk criteria to determine whether additional treatment is required Determine which residual bribery risks require additional controls or treatment based on net risk scores and defined appetite thresholds; escalate according to governance framework

1.3 The Relationship Between Standards

The three standards operate at different levels and must be understood as complementary rather than interchangeable. ISO 31000 provides the methodology. ISO 37001 specifies the subject matter and compliance requirements. ISO 37000 provides the governance framework within which the assessment must operate.

ISO 37000:2021 adds the governance layer. The governing body is accountable for defining and approving the organisation's risk appetite, and that appetite must be articulated before net risk scores can be interpreted against a threshold that triggers escalation or treatment.

Standard Role in Risk Assessment Key Contribution
ISO 31000:2018 Risk methodology framework Identification, Analysis, and Evaluation stages; context-setting; treatment options
ISO 37001:2016 Compliance requirement and scope definition Clause 4.5 mandatory factors; documentation requirements; review triggers; associated persons scope
ISO 37000:2021 Governance accountability Governing body accountability for risk appetite; oversight of assessment process; approval of treatment plans

2. The Specificity Imperative

The single most common failure in anti-bribery risk assessment is operating at the wrong level of abstraction. Organisations conduct assessments at the category level — "procurement bribery risk", "third-party risk", "permit-related risk" — and assign each category a rating. The output is a heat map. The heat map is filed. No useful action follows.

This approach fails for three reasons. First, a category-level risk cannot be controlled — controls attach to specific transactions, roles, and counterparties, not to abstract categories. Second, a category-level risk cannot be tested — there is no scenario against which a control's effectiveness can be evaluated. Third, category-level assessments consistently fail ISO 37001 certification audits, because the auditor cannot trace the risk to a specific scenario, a specific control, and evidence of that control operating.

Listing 'procurement bribery risk: HIGH' is not a risk assessment. It is a risk acknowledgement. The assessment begins where acknowledgement ends — with the specific question: who, to whom, in which transaction, using what mechanism, under what circumstances, and with what consequence if it occurs?

2.2 The Bribery Scenario: The Unit of Analysis

The fundamental unit of analysis in an anti-bribery risk assessment is the bribery scenario — not the risk category, not the risk theme, not the risk domain. The bribery scenario is a specific, plausible description of how bribery could occur in the organisation's particular context. Each scenario identifies: the role within the organisation most likely to be involved; the counterparty (who would pay or receive); the transaction or interaction in which it would occur; the form of bribe; and the intended outcome.

Bribery scenarios are inherently local. They are shaped by local business practices, local regulatory environments, local enforcement patterns, and local commercial pressures. A scenario that describes a specific risk in one jurisdiction may be implausible or entirely absent in another.

Geography Matters: the Same Company, Different Scenarios

The following illustrates the transformation from a generic risk category to specific, assessable bribery scenarios, illustrating how the same company function generates entirely different risks across jurisdictions:

Risk Category Scenario: Singapore Scenario: Indonesia Scenario: France What Makes Them Different
Government Permit Approval A regulatory officer demands an accelerated review fee (facilitation payment) for port health inspection of food imports, with risk of shipment rejection if declined. A district government official conditions grant of a mining environmental permit on the company providing employment to a family member of the official. A public procurement officer signals that a consulting contract award would be influenced by an invitation to an industry conference in Paris with travel and accommodation covered. Mechanism, counterparty type, bribe form, and leverage all differ. One standard control cannot address all three.
Commercial Decision Influence A procurement director at a state-owned enterprise requests that the company employ his spouse as a 'consultant' as a condition of contract renewal. A commercial counterparty's agent (who is undisclosed) is the nephew of the relevant minister and has no genuine commercial role beyond access provision. An official from a strategic client's parent company expects attendance at a skiing event as part of the commercial relationship, with tickets, accommodation, and entertainment at the company's expense. Each scenario implicates different control elements: employment screening, agent due diligence, or hospitality policy.

Each scenario in this table requires a different control response. None of them would be adequately addressed by a generic 'corruption risk: HIGH' rating. Each requires a specific control, a specific test of that control, and a specific treatment if the control is found inadequate.

2.3 Identifying Bribery Scenarios — The Four Lenses

Bribery scenarios can be systematically identified through four analytical lenses, applied in combination. No single lens is sufficient on its own; comprehensive scenario development requires all four.

Lens 1: Transaction Mapping

Map every significant category of transaction the organisation conducts where a decision-maker or influencer on the other side has discretion that could be influenced by a bribe. Transaction categories to map include:

  • Regulatory and licence applications (permits, approvals, registrations, certifications).
  • Public procurement (tendering, contract award, variation orders, extensions).
  • Customs and border control (clearance, valuation, tariff classification).
  • Judicial and quasi-judicial proceedings (disputes, appeals, enforcement actions).
  • Tax assessments and negotiations with revenue authorities.
  • Commercial contract award and renewal by counterparties with concentrated purchasing power.
  • Employment of individuals with regulatory influence or public official connections.

Lens 2: Interaction Mapping

Map every significant category of interaction the organisation has with persons outside the organisation who have discretion or decision-making authority affecting the organisation's interests. Interaction categories include:

  • Government and regulatory relationships: who interacts, how frequently, in what context.
  • Business partner and agent relationships: who represents the organisation and in what capacity.
  • Political relationships: donations, sponsorships, secondments, employment.
  • Charitable and community relationships: whether these could be used to channel value to officials.

Lens 3: Pressure Point Analysis

Identify the circumstances under which individuals within the organisation face pressure that could motivate or rationalise bribery. Pressure points to map include:

  • Revenue pressure: sales targets, commission structures, deal deadlines, market share requirements.
  • Operational pressure: delivery timelines, project milestones, regulatory deadline compliance.
  • Competitive pressure: markets where competitors are known or suspected to engage in bribery.
  • Relationship pressure: long-standing business relationships where refusal to pay damages the relationship.
  • Personal financial pressure: individuals in financial difficulty may be more susceptible to receiving bribes.

Lens 4: Historical and Intelligence Analysis

Analyse available evidence about how bribery has actually occurred — within the organisation (past incidents, whistleblower reports, audit findings) and externally (enforcement actions, sector intelligence). Steps include:

  • Review all internal incident reports, whistleblower submissions, and compliance findings over the past three years.
  • Analyse enforcement actions against industry peers and competitors in relevant jurisdictions.
  • Review TI CPI, TRACE Matrix, and World Bank governance indicators for each operating jurisdiction.
  • Consult local legal counsel and compliance professionals for jurisdiction-specific intelligence.
  • Review prior risk assessment findings and determine whether previously identified risks have materialised.

2.4 The Risk Register at the Right Level of Granularity

The output of the risk identification stage must be a risk register where each entry is a specific bribery scenario, not a risk category. The register must capture sufficient information to enable analysis, control mapping, and testing. The minimum required fields for each risk register entry are set out below.

Field Content Required
Risk ID Unique sequential reference (e.g., ABR-2025-014)
Scenario Title Short descriptive label — specific enough to distinguish from all other entries in the register
Scenario Description Full narrative of the bribery scenario: role of internal actor; counterparty; transaction context; bribe form; intended outcome
Jurisdiction The specific country or region where the scenario applies
Function / Process The internal business function and process within which the scenario arises
Actor (Role) The internal role most likely to commit or be subjected to bribery in this scenario
Counterparty Type Government official / Public procurement officer / Commercial decision-maker / Agent / Other
Bribe Type Cash / Gift / Hospitality / Employment / Contract award / Other financial advantage
Gross Likelihood Score 1–5 scale: likelihood of the scenario occurring in the absence of any controls, with documented rationale
Gross Consequence Score 1–5 scale: magnitude of impact if the bribery event occurs (highest dimension score across legal, financial, reputational, operational)
Gross Risk Score Likelihood × Consequence at gross level
Controls in Place Reference to each control that applies to this scenario, with control type (preventive / detective / corrective)
Control Test Result Evidence-based assessment of whether each listed control is effective, partially effective, ineffective, or not in place
Net Likelihood Score Likelihood score after applying and testing controls
Net Consequence Score Consequence score after applying controls (note: controls may not reduce consequence; hold constant unless exceptional circumstances documented)
Net Risk Score Likelihood × Consequence at net level
Risk Rating Extreme / High / Medium / Low — based on net score against risk appetite thresholds
Treatment Required Additional controls or monitoring required based on net risk rating and risk appetite comparison
Risk Owner Named individual accountable for this risk scenario
Last Reviewed Date of most recent review / assessment

3. Gross Risk: The Inherent Baseline

Gross risk — sometimes called inherent risk — is the level of exposure that exists before any controls are applied. It reflects the raw vulnerability of a given scenario given the nature of the business, the jurisdiction, the transaction, and the counterparty, assuming nothing has been done to prevent or detect the bribery.

Many organisations skip gross risk assessment entirely, proceeding directly to a net risk score that incorporates the effect of existing controls. This is a significant error. Without a documented gross risk baseline, the control framework becomes invisible: you cannot demonstrate what your controls are contributing, you cannot identify which risks are well-controlled and which are not, and you cannot defend your net risk scores under audit because there is no documented reasoning for the reduction applied.

Gross risk tells you what you are exposed to. Net risk tells you what you have done about it. The difference between them is the contribution of your controls.

Likelihood and Consequence Scales

Gross risk is calculated as the product of likelihood and consequence, each scored on a defined scale. The scales must be calibrated to the organisation's context and applied consistently across all scenarios.

Likelihood scale (1–5):

Score Label Definition Bribery-Specific Indicators
5 Almost Certain The scenario is expected to occur frequently or routinely in the absence of controls High-volume government interactions in jurisdictions where facilitation payments are routine practice; active enforcement precedent in the sector
4 Likely The scenario would occur in most circumstances without active controls Regular commercial dealings with SOEs in high-risk markets; agent-dependent business development with limited oversight
3 Possible The scenario could occur under certain conditions Periodic permit applications in moderate-risk jurisdictions; occasional government-adjacent transactions
2 Unlikely The scenario would occur only in unusual circumstances Low-frequency government interactions in jurisdictions with functional anti-corruption enforcement; strong internal culture
1 Rare The scenario would occur only in exceptional circumstances Jurisdictions with TI CPI above 75; transactions subject to independent oversight; no historic precedent in sector or organisation

Consequence scale (1–5): Consequence should be evaluated across four dimensions — legal/criminal, financial, reputational, and operational — and the highest dimension score used as the consequence rating for the scenario.

Critically, consequence assessment in an anti-bribery context must recognise that the organisation bears consequence risk on two sides: as the party that paid or facilitated a bribe (active bribery), and as the party whose personnel received or solicited a bribe (passive bribery). In both cases the legal, financial, reputational, and operational impacts can be severe. The consequence scales below apply equally to both directions.

Score Label Legal / Criminal Financial Reputational Operational
5 Catastrophic Criminal prosecution of the organisation and senior individuals; debarment from public procurement; loss of operating licences Fines exceeding 10% of annual turnover; disgorgement of profits; DPA or equivalent with ongoing monitoring obligations International media coverage; loss of major clients; board-level resignations; sustained reputational damage Operations suspended; licences revoked; loss of certification; significant disruption to core business activities
4 Major Regulatory enforcement action; significant DPA or settlement; prosecution of business unit management Fines in the range of 5–10% of annual revenue; restitution orders; significant legal and investigation costs National/regional media coverage; loss of key contracts; investor concern; ESG rating impact Significant disruption to operations in affected markets; loss of key business partner relationships
3 Significant Regulatory investigation; enforcement correspondence; remediation obligations; individual disciplinary proceedings Material legal and investigation costs; possible disgorgement of specific transaction profits; commercial loss Industry and trade media coverage; relationship damage with key counterparties; internal reputational impact Delays to projects or approvals; enhanced regulatory scrutiny; management distraction

Where consequence scores differ across dimensions (e.g., legal consequence = 3 but reputational consequence = 5), the risk register should record all dimension scores but use the highest score as the consequence input to the gross risk calculation. Applying the highest dimension score reflects the organisation's actual maximum exposure from the scenario.

The risk matrix maps the product of these two scores (1–25) to a rating band. These thresholds must be defined before scoring begins and approved by management: Extreme (≥20), High (12–19), Medium (6–11), Low (1–5).

4. Controls: Mapping, Testing, and Net Risk

Once gross risk scores are established, the assessment turns to the control framework. For each scenario, the assessor identifies every control that is in place that could reduce the likelihood that the bribery occurs, or the consequence if it does. Controls are then tested, and a net risk score is calculated based on the tested effectiveness of those controls.

4.2 The Anti-Bribery Control Taxonomy

Controls must be mapped to specific bribery scenarios. The same control can address multiple scenarios, but the mapping must be explicit: a generic listing of 'all controls' against all scenarios provides no analytical value. The taxonomy below provides a comprehensive reference for the types of controls available in an anti-bribery control framework.

Type Control Category Examples What It Reduces
PREVENTIVE CONTROLS
Preventive Policy & Documentation Anti-bribery policy; gifts & hospitality policy; facilitation payments prohibition; conflicts of interest policy; political contributions policy Likelihood — removes ambiguity about what is and is not permitted; creates a documented standard against which conduct is assessed
Preventive Training & Awareness Role-specific ABMS training; scenario-based learning; management certification; annual refresher training; pre-travel briefings for high-risk jurisdictions Likelihood — increases individual awareness of scenarios and reduces rationalisation of bribery as acceptable or unavoidable
Preventive Due Diligence Business partner due diligence; agent screening; JV partner assessment; supplier onboarding checks; PEP screening; sanctions screening Likelihood — filters out high-risk counterparties before engagement; surfaces red flags that require additional controls or rejection
Preventive Authorisation & Segregation Dual-approval for high-value transactions; segregation of duties in procurement; pre-approval requirements for gifts, hospitality, and donations; government interaction authorisation protocols Likelihood — removes single-point-of-failure decision authority; requires a second person to approve before a potentially corrupt transaction can proceed
Preventive Contractual Controls Anti-bribery representations and warranties; audit rights clauses; termination rights for bribery breaches; compliance certification requirements in agent/partner contracts Likelihood — extends compliance obligations to third parties; creates contractual basis for investigation and termination if bribery is suspected or confirmed
DETECTIVE CONTROLS
Detective Monitoring & Analytics Expense claim analysis; gifts & hospitality register review; payment anomaly detection; third-party payment monitoring; agent commission monitoring Likelihood of recurrence — creates detection capability that deters bribery by increasing the probability that it will be identified
Detective Reporting Channels Whistleblower hotline; ethics reporting mechanisms; manager escalation protocols; speak-up culture initiatives Likelihood of recurrence — creates detection mechanism for events that do not surface through monitoring; provides early warning of emerging risk scenarios
Detective Audit Internal audit of ABMS controls; transaction testing; third-party payment audits; due diligence file audits; joint venture audits Likelihood of recurrence — identifies control failures before they escalate; provides objective assurance on control effectiveness
Detective Management Review Periodic ABMS performance review; KPI monitoring; risk indicator dashboards; compliance committee oversight; board reporting on ABMS Likelihood of recurrence — senior management visibility creates accountability and enables early intervention when trends indicate elevated exposure
CORRECTIVE CONTROLS
Corrective Investigation Internal investigation capability or protocol; external investigation support; legal privilege protocols; evidence preservation procedures Consequence — limits extent of harm by enabling rapid, competent response to suspected bribery events
Corrective Disciplinary Framework Documented disciplinary process for ABMS breaches; proportionate sanctions up to and including termination; board-level notification for senior personnel Consequence — deters through credible enforcement; enables proportionate response; supports regulatory self-reporting narratives
Corrective Remediation Non-conformity management; corrective action process; voluntary disclosure protocols; regulatory engagement procedures; third-party contract termination Consequence — reduces downstream legal and regulatory consequence by demonstrating proactive response and systemic remediation

An untested control is a hypothesis. When you record a net risk score based on controls that have not been tested, you are not assessing risk — you are expressing hope.

4.3 Control Testing: Methodology and Evidence

Control testing is the process of gathering objective evidence that a control is operating as designed, with sufficient consistency to justify a credit in the net risk calculation. Each control must be evaluated against three questions before it can be credited in the net risk calculation:

Design Adequacy

Is the control designed to address the risk?

A control that is well-designed but addresses a different risk, or that has no logical connection to the bribery scenario, cannot reduce the net risk for that scenario. Design adequacy is assessed by reviewing the control's documented purpose, scope, and mechanism.

Implementation

Has the control been implemented as designed?

A policy that exists on paper but has never been communicated, trained, or operationalised is not a functioning control. Implementation testing requires evidence that the control exists in the real operating environment — not just in the policy library.

Operating Effectiveness

Is the control operating effectively over time?

Operating effectiveness testing requires evidence of actual performance: approval records, training completion logs, due diligence files, exception reports, audit findings. A control that operated effectively six months ago may not be operating effectively today.

The following table sets out the recommended testing approach, evidence of effectiveness, and key red flags for the eight most commonly relied-upon anti-bribery controls:

Anti-Bribery Policy Document review; version control check; staff awareness testing Policy reviewed in last 12 months; distributed to all covered persons; accessible; staff can describe key prohibitions Policy has not been reviewed since certification; staff unaware of policy content or location; no distribution records

4.4 Control Effectiveness Ratings

Control testing must produce a documented effectiveness rating for each control tested. The rating directly determines the extent to which likelihood can be reduced from gross to net. These ratings determine how much the gross risk score is reduced:

Rating Definition Effect on Net Likelihood Evidence Required
EFFECTIVE Control is designed adequately, implemented as designed, and operating consistently with objective evidence of performance Likelihood score may be reduced by 2–3 points from gross level Positive results from all three test questions (design, implementation, operating effectiveness); contemporaneous evidence available
PARTIALLY EFFECTIVE Control is designed adequately and partially implemented but testing reveals gaps in operating effectiveness or coverage Likelihood score may be reduced by 1 point from gross level Mixed test results; implementation gaps documented; corrective action identified
INEFFECTIVE Control exists on paper but testing reveals it is not consistently operating as designed No reduction from gross likelihood — net likelihood = gross likelihood for this control Test results show systematic implementation failure; multiple gaps identified; remediation required before credit can be taken
NOT IN PLACE Control does not exist. No relevant policy, procedure, system, or process is in place to address this scenario Not applicable — gross and net likelihood are identical; control gap triggers treatment requirement N/A — absence of control is itself the evidence

A critical point: controls primarily reduce likelihood, not consequence. The consequence of a bribery event — prosecution, financial penalty, reputational damage — is determined by the nature of the act, the jurisdiction, and enforcement practice, not by the organisation's internal control framework. Adjusting consequence scores downward based on the existence of controls is a methodological error that inflates optimism and deflates treatment urgency.

This distinction has important implications. An organisation with very high inherent consequence exposure (Catastrophic, score 5) will retain significant net risk even when controls are highly effective, because consequence is not reduced. This is not a failure of the control framework — it is an accurate reflection of the nature of bribery exposure.

4.5 Gross to Net: Worked Example

Assessment Element Gross Risk Net Risk
Scenario Sales representative in Vietnam may offer cash to a government procurement official to secure a contract award during tender evaluation period ← same scenario
Likelihood Score 4 (Likely) — Government procurement in Vietnam; volume of interactions; agent-dependent; limited oversight of representative conduct 2 (Unlikely) — Specific policy prohibition; role-specific Vietnam pre-travel briefing; dual-approval for contract bids over USD 50,000; ABCO oversight
Consequence Score 5 (Catastrophic) — Vietnam FCPA/UKBA exposure; potential prosecution of organisation and individuals; debarment risk; reputational impact 5 (Catastrophic) — Controls reduce likelihood but not consequence. If the bribery occurs despite controls, legal exposure is unchanged
Risk Score 4 × 5 = 20 (EXTREME) 2 × 5 = 10 (HIGH)
Controls Relied Upon N/A at gross stage (1) ABMS Policy — EFFECTIVE; (2) Vietnam Pre-Travel Briefing — EFFECTIVE; (3) Dual-Approval Process — EFFECTIVE; (4) Expense Monitoring — PARTIALLY EFFECTIVE
Control Gap Identified N/A Expense monitoring partially effective — cash expenditure in Hanoi office not systematically reviewed. Treatment required: implement monthly cash reconciliation review by Finance with ABCO escalation threshold

Note that in this example, despite four controls being in place (three effective, one partially effective), the net risk remains HIGH (not MEDIUM or LOW). This is because consequence = 5 (Catastrophic) cannot be reduced by controls, and the partially effective monitoring leaves a residual likelihood above what fully effective controls would achieve. The treatment plan addresses the partially effective control specifically.

5. Risk Evaluation and Treatment

5.1 Risk Appetite and Evaluation Criteria

Risk evaluation — the comparison of net risk scores against defined criteria to determine acceptability — cannot be conducted without a defined risk appetite. Risk appetite is the statement of how much risk the organisation is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. In an anti-bribery context, risk appetite has a particular character: it is almost always expressed as zero tolerance for bribery itself (the event), combined with a tolerance threshold for the level of residual control risk the organisation is willing to carry.

This distinction is critical. An organisation that says 'we have zero tolerance for bribery' is stating its ethical position. An organisation that defines its risk appetite for anti-bribery risk is making a governance decision about how much residual risk it accepts, how much monitoring it applies to HIGH-rated risks, and at what net risk level it requires escalation to the Board or Audit Committee.

Risk appetite must be defined by the governing body (consistent with ISO 37000) before the risk evaluation stage. It should specify at minimum: the net risk rating levels at which scenarios are automatically escalated to top management; the net risk rating levels at which additional treatment is required; and the net risk rating levels that are within the organisation's accepted tolerance for ongoing monitoring without further immediate action.

Net Risk Rating Typical Appetite Position Management Response Required Reporting Level
EXTREME (≥20) Outside all appetite — immediate action required Immediate escalation; mandatory additional controls; enhanced monitoring; board-level reporting Board / Governing Body; Anti-Bribery Compliance Officer
HIGH (12–19) Outside normal appetite — treatment required before acceptance Senior management review; defined treatment plan with timelines; quarterly progress tracking; internal audit coverage Senior Management; Compliance Committee; ABCO
MEDIUM (6–11) At the boundary of appetite — monitoring required Annual treatment plan review; enhanced monitoring of controls; documented in risk register with named owner Management Review; ABCO
LOW (1–5) Within appetite — standard monitoring Standard monitoring; annual risk register review; no additional treatment required unless appetite changes Annual Risk Assessment; ABCO review

5.2 The Four Treatment Options

Avoid

Risk avoidance means eliminating the activity that gives rise to the bribery risk. In practice this means declining to enter a market, refusing to engage a category of business partner, withdrawing from a transaction type, or exiting a jurisdiction where the risk cannot be reduced to an acceptable level through controls. Risk avoidance is the only treatment option that genuinely eliminates the scenario rather than managing it. It is appropriate for EXTREME net risk scenarios where controls cannot bring the risk within appetite and the business value of the activity does not justify the exposure.

Reduce

Risk reduction means implementing additional or enhanced controls to bring net risk within appetite. This is the most common treatment response and the one most directly connected to the ISO 37001 control framework. Reduction treatments should be specific, actionable, and time-bound. A treatment plan that says 'strengthen compliance culture' is not a treatment — it is an aspiration. A treatment plan that says 'implement quarterly pre-travel briefings for all sales staff travelling to Indonesia, with sign-off by ABCO, by 31 March 2025' is a treatment.

Share / Transfer

Risk sharing transfers some portion of the financial consequence to a third party — typically through insurance (Directors and Officers liability; legal expense coverage for regulatory investigations; anti-bribery specific coverage products) or through contractual allocation of risk with partners. Risk sharing does not reduce the likelihood of bribery occurring, nor does it eliminate legal consequence. It is a financial mitigation tool, not a compliance control. It is appropriate as a supplementary treatment for scenarios where the financial dimension of consequence has been assessed as significant and the organisation has brought likelihood within appetite but wishes to limit financial exposure from residual events.

Accept

Risk acceptance means deciding that a risk at its current net level falls within the organisation's risk appetite and does not require additional treatment at this time. Acceptance must be an explicit, documented, and authorised decision — not an absence of action. The risk register must record: the net risk score, the treatment decision (accept), the authorising person or body, the date of the decision, and the next scheduled review. Acceptance without documentation is not a governance decision — it is negligence.

5.3 Treatment Plans: From Decision to Action

Every net risk scenario rated above the acceptance threshold must have a documented treatment plan. The treatment plan bridges the gap between the risk evaluation decision and the operational actions required to improve the control environment. Treatment plans must be specific enough to be auditable. The minimum required fields for a treatment plan are:

Field Requirement
Treatment Plan ID Reference number linked to the risk register entry
Risk Reference ABR-ID of the scenario being treated
Current Net Risk Rating Rating triggering the treatment requirement
Target Net Risk Rating The rating the organisation aims to achieve after treatment
Treatment Option Avoid / Reduce / Share / Accept
Specific Actions Each action described with enough specificity to be verified — control type, scope, coverage, mechanism
Responsible Owner Named individual for each action
Target Completion Date Realistic date; not 'ongoing'
Resource Requirements Budget, personnel, or system changes required
Progress Milestone(s) Interim checkpoints to verify progress before final completion date
Evidence of Completion Specific artefacts that will demonstrate the action has been completed (e.g., training records, system screenshots, updated policy version)
Post-Treatment Testing Date When the control will be tested following implementation to verify it is operating as designed
Approved By Authorising person; must be at a level commensurate with the net risk rating being treated

Treatment plans must be specific, actionable, time-bound, and auditable. The risk assessment is not complete until every risk above the organisation's appetite threshold has a documented treatment plan with a named owner and a delivery date.

These are not templates to be adopted without adaptation — they are starting points for the scenario development work that each organisation must conduct based on its own specific context, transactions, and operating environment. In every case, the organisation must localise and refine these scenarios to reflect the actual specifics of its people, processes, counterparties, and jurisdictions.

7. Conducting the Risk Assessment: Process and Governance

7.1 Who Should Conduct the Assessment

The anti-bribery risk assessment must be led by a person with the authority, competence, and independence to conduct it credibly. ISO 37001 Clause 5.3 requires the designation of an Anti-Bribery Compliance Officer (ABCO) or equivalent. The ABCO is typically responsible for leading the risk assessment, though the assessment requires inputs from across the organisation and cannot be conducted in isolation.

The assessment process should involve the following participants:

ABCO or Equivalent

Overall responsibility for assessment design, methodology, facilitation, and sign-off. The ABCO must have sufficient seniority and independence to probe sensitive areas, challenge business unit assumptions, and document risks accurately regardless of commercial implications.

Legal Counsel

Jurisdiction-specific legal risk assessment; regulatory enforcement analysis; privilege considerations for investigation-sensitive scenarios. Legal Counsel provides context on the applicable legal framework and the consequences of identified scenarios under relevant legislation.

Senior Business Line Managers

Identification of business-specific scenarios; transaction context; commercial pressure points; counterparty intelligence. Business line managers provide the operational knowledge that transforms abstract scenario categories into specific, plausible risks.

Finance and Treasury

Financial flows; payment mechanisms; expense monitoring capability. Finance provides insight into payment channels, petty cash usage, agent commission structures, and expense claim patterns that may indicate risk or control gaps.

Human Resources

Compensation structure analysis; employee pressure indicators; training delivery. HR provides insight into incentive structures that could create bribery pressure, and supports the delivery of role-specific training as a preventive control.

Procurement

Supplier and vendor relationships; procurement process design; conflict of interest exposure. Procurement is both a subject of risk assessment (inbound bribery through vendor kickbacks) and a contributor of controls (competitive tendering, segregation of duties).

Internal Audit

Control testing methodology; prior findings; audit programme integration. Internal Audit provides objectivity in control testing and integrates risk assessment findings into the annual audit plan to ensure ongoing monitoring coverage.

Operations / Country Managers

Jurisdiction-specific intelligence; local relationship context; operational pressure points. Country managers provide ground-level knowledge of local corruption practices, counterparty behaviour, and the practical reality of operating in specific markets.

For organisations with significant international operations, local management and local legal counsel in each operating jurisdiction should be engaged for that jurisdiction's scenario development and context analysis. An assessment conducted entirely from headquarters, without input from those operating in the relevant markets, will systematically underestimate jurisdiction-specific risks.

7.2 Assessment Process and Timeline

The risk assessment is not a desk exercise. It requires structured engagement with business units, operational functions, and — where applicable — associated persons. The following 12-step process reflects the requirements of ISO 37001 Clause 4.5 and the methodology of ISO 31000.

Step Stage Activities Output
1 Scope Definition Define the entities, geographies, functions, and transaction types in scope. Confirm alignment with ABMS scope. Identify assessment team. Obtain management approval for methodology. Scope document; risk appetite statement; risk criteria approved by governing body
2 Context Analysis Review organisational context (ISO 37001 Clause 4.1); internal context (business model, structure, transaction types); external context (jurisdictions, enforcement, CPI data, sector intelligence) Context analysis document; jurisdiction risk profiles; incident history summary
3 Transaction and Interaction Mapping Map all relevant transaction types and interaction categories across functions and geographies using the four lenses Transaction and interaction inventory
4 Scenario Development For each transaction/interaction type and geography, develop specific bribery scenarios with actor, counterparty, bribe form, and circumstance Draft bribery scenario library
5 Gross Risk Scoring Score each scenario for gross likelihood and gross consequence (all four dimensions). Calculate gross risk score and assign gross risk rating. Gross risk register with scores and rationale
6 Control Mapping Map existing anti-bribery controls to each scenario. Document control type, design, and scope for each mapped control. Risk register with control mapping
7 Control Testing Test the design adequacy, implementation, and operational effectiveness of each mapped control. Document testing methodology and evidence. Control testing log; effectiveness ratings; gap register
8 Net Risk Scoring Apply control effectiveness ratings to gross scores to calculate net likelihood. Calculate net risk score and assign net risk rating. Net risk register; residual risk map
9 Risk Evaluation Compare net risk scores against risk appetite and risk criteria. Identify scenarios requiring treatment. Risk evaluation summary; prioritised treatment list
10 Treatment Planning Develop specific treatment plans for all scenarios outside appetite. Document owner, deadline, and evidence of completion for each action. Treatment plans; updated control programme
11 Documentation and Approval Compile the full risk assessment document for approval by senior management and, where required, the governing body. Approved risk assessment; Board or Management Committee minutes recording approval
12 Integration and Monitoring Integrate risk assessment outputs into internal audit plan, training programme, due diligence scope, and monitoring framework. Establish review schedule. Integrated ABMS programme; monitoring calendar; next review date confirmed

7.3 Review Frequency and Triggers

ISO 37001 Clause 4.5 requires the risk assessment to be reviewed at planned intervals and whenever significant changes occur. 'Planned intervals' should be interpreted as at least annually for the full assessment, with interim reviews triggered by specific events. The following events should trigger an unscheduled review:

  • Entry into a new market, jurisdiction, or sector.
  • Acquisition of or merger with another organisation.
  • Commencement of material new business relationships (agents, JV partners, significant suppliers).
  • A bribery incident or credible allegation within the organisation.
  • A significant enforcement action against a peer organisation in the same market or sector.
  • A material change in the regulatory environment of an operating jurisdiction.
  • A significant restructuring of the organisation's operations or business model.
  • Identification of a significant control failure through audit, monitoring, or other source.

The review schedule and trigger events must be documented in the risk assessment procedure. It is insufficient to rely on an annual calendar reminder. The ABCO must maintain active awareness of external developments and internal changes that could affect the validity of the current risk assessment.

8. Common Failures in Anti-Bribery Risk Assessment

The following failures are consistently observed in risk assessments reviewed during ISO 37001 gap analyses, certification pre-audits, and post-incident investigations. Each represents a methodological or governance deficiency that undermines the value and defensibility of the assessment.

Failure Why It Undermines the Assessment What a Sound Assessment Does Instead
Assessing at category level rather than scenario level Categories cannot be controlled, tested, or audited. 'Government interaction risk: HIGH' does not tell you which government, which transaction, which internal role, or what bribe form to address. Every risk register entry is a specific, plausible bribery scenario with a named actor, counterparty, transaction, bribe form, and jurisdiction
Applying the same risk profile across all jurisdictions Bribery risk is local. Vietnam, Singapore, France, and Nigeria present entirely different scenarios, mechanisms, and control requirements. A single profile is almost certainly wrong in every jurisdiction. Jurisdiction-specific scenario development; local management and legal counsel engaged for each material geography; jurisdiction risk profiles documented
Conflating gross and net risk If you only see net risk, you cannot evaluate whether your controls are adequate or whether they are actually working. The gross-to-net gap is exactly where the control framework lives. Explicit gross scoring stage before controls are introduced; gross scores documented with rationale; control credit applied only after testing
Recording controls without testing them An untested control is a documented intention. Net risk scores based on untested controls are assessments of aspiration, not reality. They will not survive audit scrutiny. Every control relied upon in net risk scoring must have been tested for design adequacy, implementation, and operating effectiveness before the credit is applied
Assuming controls reduce consequence as well as likelihood Controls primarily reduce likelihood. If bribery occurs despite controls, the legal, financial, and reputational consequence is determined by the act — not by whether the organisation had a policy prohibiting it. Consequence scores are assessed independently of controls. Control credit is applied only to likelihood. Consequence is held constant unless exceptional and documented circumstances justify an adjustment.
Treating risk acceptance as absence of action Risks 'accepted' without documentation or authorisation represent undocumented governance decisions. If the risk materialises, the organisation cannot demonstrate that the acceptance was a considered decision rather than negligence. Acceptance decisions are explicitly documented, authorised at a level commensurate with the rating, and scheduled for review
Not assessing passive/inbound bribery ISO 37001 covers both active and passive bribery. Organisations that assess only outbound bribery systematically underestimate their exposure to procurement kickbacks, management conflicts of interest, and corruption of the control framework itself. Risk register includes scenarios for staff receiving bribes, undisclosed interests, and corruption of internal controls
Failing to connect risk assessment to control design Risk assessment produced as a standalone document with no visible connection to the control programme, training curriculum, audit plan, or due diligence scope has no operational value. It is a compliance document, not a governance tool. Risk assessment directly drives: control programme design; training content and targeting; audit scope; due diligence calibration; monitoring focus
No review following significant events Risk assessment becomes stale as the business changes. Controls that were adequate when the assessment was conducted may no longer be in place; new scenarios may have emerged; the risk profile may have changed materially. Documented trigger-based review protocol; ABCO monitors for trigger events; review completed and documented when triggers occur
Treatment plans that are generic or aspirational 'Enhance compliance culture' or 'increase training' are not treatment plans. They cannot be tracked, tested, or held to account. An auditor will reject them. Treatment plans specify exact actions, responsible owners, completion dates, and evidence of completion

9. What an ISO 37001 Auditor Will Look For

Understanding how an ISO 37001 certification auditor will approach the risk assessment review is essential for organisations preparing for certification or maintaining ongoing compliance. An auditor's job is not to find a risk assessment document — it is to determine whether the organisation actually understands its bribery risks and has built controls that actually work. Auditors assess the risk assessment across three evidence categories.

9.1 Document Review — What Auditors Will Read

  • The risk assessment procedure: does it define methodology, frequency, roles, and trigger events?
  • The risk register: does it contain specific scenarios (not categories)? Does it have gross and net scores? Are controls identified and rated?
  • Control testing documentation: what was tested, when, by whom, and what did the testing find?
  • Treatment plans: are they specific, owned, time-bound, and linked to risk register entries?
  • Approval records: has the risk assessment been formally approved by the appropriate level of management?
  • Review records: is there evidence of the last review? Were trigger events assessed?

9.2 Interview Evidence — What Auditors Will Ask

  • "Walk me through how you identified the bribery scenarios in your risk register." — Tests whether the process was genuinely substantive or whether scenarios were imported from a template without contextual work.
  • "What is your gross likelihood score for this scenario, and why?" — Tests whether assessors understand the gross/net distinction and can articulate the reasoning behind scores.
  • "How did you test this control, and what did the testing find?" — Tests whether control testing is genuine and evidenced, or whether it is a documentation exercise.
  • "Show me the evidence that this control is operating effectively." — Forces production of test documentation. If it cannot be produced, the control credit is unsupported.
  • "What would happen to your net risk score if this control failed?" — Tests understanding of control dependency and risk sensitivity.
  • "Has there been any event since your last review that should have triggered a reassessment?" — Tests whether the review trigger mechanism is actually applied or merely documented.

9.3 Transaction Testing — What Auditors Will Sample

  • A sample of gifts and hospitality register entries: are entries complete, approved, and consistent with policy limits? Any patterns of fragmentation or missing entries?
  • A sample of business partner due diligence files: are they complete? Proportionate to risk? Free of unresolved red flags?
  • A sample of high-risk transactions (government-adjacent sales, public procurement bids, agent-facilitated deals): do they follow the documented controls?
  • Expense claim data for high-risk roles in high-risk jurisdictions: any anomalies consistent with facilitation payment patterns?

An auditor's job is not to find a risk assessment document. It is to determine whether the organisation actually knows what its bribery risks are, and whether it has built controls that actually work.

10. Conclusion

Anti-bribery risk assessment under ISO 37001 Clause 4.5 is not a compliance exercise to be completed once and filed. It is the analytical foundation on which every other element of the ABMS depends. The due diligence scope, the training content, the monitoring focus, the audit programme, and the treatment priorities should all derive directly from what the risk assessment reveals.

The assessment must be specific. Bribery occurs in specific transactions, conducted by specific people, with specific counterparties, using specific mechanisms. An assessment that does not reach that level of specificity cannot inform the specific controls required to prevent it.

The assessment must distinguish between gross and net risk. Gross risk tells you what you are exposed to before controls. Net risk tells you what you have done about it. The gap between them is the entire contribution of the control framework. Without that distinction, the assessment cannot demonstrate whether controls are adequate, calibrated, or working.

Control testing is not optional. It is the mechanism by which claimed risk reduction becomes evidenced risk reduction. Every control credited in the net risk calculation must have been tested — for design, implementation, and operating effectiveness — before the credit is applied. An untested control is a hypothesis, not a control.

The organisations that build their ABMS on a genuinely rigorous risk assessment — specific, methodologically sound, gross-to-net, and control-tested — will find that it delivers governance value that extends well beyond ISO 37001 certification. It provides a defensible account of the organisation's actual exposure, a clear and honest assessment of where the control framework is strong and where it is not, and a governance record that demonstrates to regulators, prosecutors, and boards alike that anti-bribery compliance is a genuine organisational discipline rather than a documentation exercise.

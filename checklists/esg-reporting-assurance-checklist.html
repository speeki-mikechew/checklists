ESG Reporting Assurance Readiness Checklist

Whether you're reporting for CSRD (ESRS), IFRS S1 and S2, GRI or TCFD, this checklist explains what assurance providers evaluate and the documentation standards they apply when conducting external assurance engagements.

Governance and Oversight

What Auditors Look For

Auditors expect ESG reporting to have the same governance rigour as financial reporting, including board-level oversight, clear accountability, and documented policies.

Red flag: ESG metrics not subject to the same controls as financial reporting. No audit committee involvement in sustainability disclosures. Unclear accountability for data quality.
Why this matters: Strong governance signals organisational commitment to reliable reporting and creates the foundation for successful assurance engagements.
2

Data Collection and Reporting

What Auditors Look For

Data quality is the foundation of credible ESG reporting. Auditors verify source documentation, calculation methodologies, and consistency of data collection across the organisation.

Red flag: Manual data aggregation from multiple disconnected sources. KPIs calculated differently across business units. No defined data ownership or accountability structure.
Why this matters: Assurance providers must be able to trace reported figures back to source data. Inconsistent collection methods make verification difficult or impossible.
3

Gap Analysis and Readiness Assessment

What Auditors Look For

Auditors value organisations that proactively identify and address weaknesses before the assurance engagement. A gap analysis demonstrates maturity and self-awareness.

Red flag: No gap analysis performed prior to assurance engagement. Repeat findings from previous assessments without documented remediation. Targets set without documented baseline methodology.
Why this matters: Gap analysis helps identify issues early, reducing scope for adverse findings and demonstrating a commitment to continuous improvement.
4

Documentation and Audit Trail

What Auditors Look For

Complete audit trails are essential for limited and reasonable assurance. Auditors require evidence of how data was collected, calculated, reviewed, and approved at each stage.

Red flag: Historical data restated without documented rationale. Methodologies and assumptions not recorded. Evidence files organised ad-hoc rather than systematically.
Why this matters: Without proper documentation, auditors cannot provide assurance. A clear audit trail demonstrates control over the reporting process.
5

Pre-Assurance Activities

What Auditors Look For

Pre-assurance preparation demonstrates readiness. Auditors appreciate working with organisations that have tested their disclosures and trained their teams on what to expect.

Red flag: No internal review or second-line review of ESG disclosures before assurance engagement. Teams unfamiliar with assurance requirements or interview processes.
Why this matters: Pre-assurance activities reduce friction during the engagement and demonstrate organisational maturity in managing ESG reporting.
6

Assurance Provider Selection and Next Steps

What Auditors Look For

The right assurance provider will understand your industry and reporting requirements. Auditors also evaluate whether you've prepared your team to work efficiently during the engagement.

Red flag: No clear understanding of limited vs reasonable assurance requirements. Provider selection based solely on cost without considering expertise. No single point of contact for assurance provider requests.
Why this matters: A well-managed assurance engagement is more efficient and less costly. Proper preparation ensures your organisation can respond promptly to provider requests.

What Auditors Look For: ESG Reporting Assurance Red Flags

When conducting ESG reporting assurance engagements, auditors commonly flag the following data management, governance, and disclosure concerns.

Data Management Red Flags
  • Manual data aggregation from multiple disconnected sources
  • No defined data ownership or accountability structure
  • KPIs calculated differently across business units
  • Historical data restated without documented rationale
Governance Red Flags
  • ESG metrics not subject to same controls as financial reporting
  • Materiality assessment conducted without stakeholder input documentation
  • Targets set without documented baseline methodology
  • No internal audit or second-line review of ESG disclosures
Disclosure Red Flags
  • Forward-looking statements without stated assumptions
  • Peer comparisons using non-standardised metrics
  • Environmental claims not tied to verified programmes
  • Social metrics lacking demographic or geographic disaggregation

01. Governance, Strategy and Policy

What Auditors Look For

GHG reporting must have clear governance and accountability. Auditors expect to see board-level oversight, documented policies, and defined roles similar to financial reporting controls.

Red flag: No documented climate policy or board oversight. GHG reporting treated as a side project without dedicated resources. Unclear accountability for emissions data quality.
Why this matters: Under ISAE 3410, auditors assess whether management has appropriate governance over the GHG statement. Weak governance undermines confidence in reported figures.

02. Scope and Methodology

What Auditors Look For

Auditors verify that your scope definition and methodology are appropriate, documented, and applied consistently. They expect clear rationale for included and excluded categories.

Red flag: Incomplete scope documentation. Scope 3 categories excluded without documented rationale. Inconsistent boundary definitions across business units or reporting periods.
Why this matters: Scope and methodology decisions directly affect reported emissions. Auditors must understand and validate these choices to provide assurance.

03. Data and Teams

What Auditors Look For

Data quality is critical for GHG assurance. Auditors verify source documentation, test calculation accuracy, and assess whether data collection processes are robust and repeatable.

Red flag: Data collected manually from multiple disconnected sources. No clear data ownership for each emissions category. Activity data cannot be traced to source documentation.
Why this matters: Auditors sample test emissions data back to source documents. Without robust data collection processes, findings often include data quality limitations.

04. Reporting and Improvement

What Auditors Look For

Auditors assess your reporting processes, reduction targets, and framework alignment. They expect to see documented review procedures and evidence of continuous improvement.

Red flag: Targets set without documented baseline methodology. No internal review process before external disclosure. Emission factors selected without documented rationale.
Why this matters: Mature reporting processes demonstrate organisational commitment and reduce the risk of material misstatement in your GHG statement.

05. Tools and Systems

What Auditors Look For

Auditors evaluate whether your systems support data integrity, audit trails, and efficient evidence provision. Centralised systems with built-in controls facilitate smoother assurance engagements.

Red flag: Emissions calculated in disconnected spreadsheets. No system controls to prevent data manipulation. Audit trails incomplete or non-existent.
Why this matters: Robust systems reduce the risk of errors and make it easier for auditors to verify your GHG statement. Poor systems often lead to scope limitations in assurance reports.

What Auditors Look For: GHG Reporting Red Flags

When providing limited or reasonable assurance over GHG emissions under ISAE 3410, auditors commonly flag the following data quality, methodology, and process concerns.

Data Quality Red Flags
  • Scope 3 calculations using industry averages for >80% of emissions
  • No documentation of emission factor sources or vintage
  • Activity data from self-reported supplier questionnaires without verification
  • Year-on-year intensity improvements exceeding 15% without documented initiatives
Methodology Red Flags
  • Inconsistent organisational boundaries between years
  • Market-based scope 2 claims without valid contractual instruments
  • Carbon offset retirements claimed before project verification
  • Biogenic emissions not separately disclosed
Process Red Flags
  • No formal review or sign-off process for emissions data
  • Calculation spreadsheets without version control or change logs
  • Lack of reconciliation between financial and operational control boundaries

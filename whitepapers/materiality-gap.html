1. Executive Summary

Materiality assessments sit at the foundation of every credible sustainability programme. They determine which ESG topics a company will prioritise, what metrics it will track, where it will allocate resources, and what it will ultimately report to stakeholders, regulators and assurance providers.

In recent years, a growing number of companies have turned to external data providers and consultants to produce these assessments. These providers collect company inputs such as sector classifications, geographic footprints, operational profiles and product portfolios, feed them through proprietary models and algorithms, and deliver a finished materiality assessment document. In many cases, companies receive this output and proceed directly to implementation, treating the external assessment as the definitive answer rather than what it should be: an informed starting point.

This whitepaper argues that this approach introduces significant risk. Based on our experience across hundreds of ESG assurance engagements, we consistently find that externally produced materiality assessments are approximately 75% accurate when implemented without internal review. That remaining 25% creates serious downstream consequences: misaligned sustainability programmes, wasted reporting effort, metrics built on irrelevant topics, and material gaps that surface during assurance.

The output from a data provider or consultant should be treated as an input to your internal process, not as the process itself.

2. The Rise of Data-Driven Materiality Assessments

The sustainability landscape has changed dramatically over the past five years. The introduction of mandatory reporting frameworks such as the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) standards, and various national regulations has created enormous demand for materiality assessments. Companies that previously had no formal sustainability programme now need robust double materiality assessments, often within tight timelines.

This demand has spawned a rapidly growing market of ESG data providers. These companies offer technology-driven platforms that promise to simplify and accelerate the materiality assessment process. The typical model works as follows:

Company Inputs

The company provides basic operational inputs: industry classification, geographic locations, number of employees, product or service categories, and key supply chain characteristics.

Platform Processing

The platform processes these inputs against proprietary databases, sector benchmarks, regulatory mapping tools, and sometimes AI-driven models.

Assessment Output

A materiality assessment document is generated, typically identifying and ranking the sustainability topics considered material to the company.

Delivery

The company receives the output, often with some supporting data and rationale.

The "Data Only" Positioning

Many of these providers deliberately position themselves as "data platforms" rather than consulting firms. This distinction is often commercial and regulatory in nature: by claiming to provide data rather than advisory services, they seek to avoid the obligations and liability associated with consulting engagements. Yet the end product is not raw data. It is a structured, prioritised materiality assessment — a document that makes explicit judgements about which sustainability topics matter most to a specific company.

The practical effect is that companies receive what looks and functions like a consultant's deliverable but without the contextual guidance, challenge sessions, and tailored expertise that a genuine consulting engagement would involve. The document arrives with an implicit authority that discourages questioning.

3. The 75% Problem: Where External Assessments Fall Short

Based on our extensive assurance work, we have observed a consistent pattern: when companies implement an externally produced materiality assessment without meaningful internal review, the result is typically around 75% accurate. While that may sound acceptable at first glance, the implications of the remaining 25% are far-reaching.

Common Gaps and Errors

Misidentified Material Topics

Data providers work from sector averages and generic operational profiles. They cannot account for the specific nuances of a company's business model, strategic direction, stakeholder expectations, or risk appetite. A topic that is genuinely material to one company in a sector may be largely irrelevant to another, and vice versa.

Missing Material Topics

Algorithms and databases have blind spots. Emerging risks, localised environmental or social issues, company-specific supply chain vulnerabilities, and topics driven by recent strategic decisions often fall outside the scope of standardised models. These omissions can represent the most significant gaps.

Incorrect Prioritisation

Even when the right topics are identified, their relative importance is frequently misjudged. A data provider may rank a topic as high priority based on sector benchmarks, while the company's actual exposure, based on its specific operations and value chain, may be low. The reverse is equally common.

Lack of Double Materiality Depth

Under frameworks like CSRD, companies must assess both impact materiality (how the company affects people and planet) and financial materiality (how sustainability issues affect the company financially). Many data-driven assessments handle one dimension adequately but treat the other superficially.

Stakeholder Disconnect

A credible materiality assessment requires meaningful stakeholder engagement. Data platforms cannot replicate the depth of insight that comes from engaging directly with employees, customers, suppliers, investors, regulators, and affected communities. Proxy data and industry surveys are not substitutes for genuine dialogue.

The Assurance Reality Check

In our audit engagements, we frequently encounter companies that have built entire sustainability programmes on unreviewed external assessments. When we challenge a particular metric or target, the response is often: "This is what the data provider said was material." When we then ask why the company is investing significant resources tracking and reporting on a topic that has minimal actual relevance to their operations, the answer is silence. The assessment was never questioned.

4. The Downstream Consequences

A materiality assessment is not an end in itself. It is the foundation upon which a company builds its entire sustainability architecture: strategy, targets, metrics, data collection processes, governance structures, and external reporting. When that foundation is 25% wrong, every layer built on top of it inherits and amplifies those errors.

Wasted Resources

Companies allocate significant time, budget, and personnel to sustainability programmes. When topics are incorrectly identified as material, those resources are misdirected. Teams spend months collecting data, setting baselines, and building reporting processes for topics that do not warrant that level of attention, while genuinely material topics receive insufficient focus.

Weak Reporting and Disclosure

Sustainability reports built on flawed materiality assessments contain disclosures that do not reflect the company's true sustainability profile. This creates problems with regulators who expect materiality-driven reporting, with investors who rely on disclosures to inform capital allocation, and with rating agencies that assess the quality and relevance of reported information.

Assurance Failures

As sustainability assurance moves from voluntary to mandatory under frameworks like CSRD, the quality of the materiality assessment becomes directly relevant to the assurance engagement. Companies that cannot demonstrate a robust internal review process face qualified opinions, increased audit costs, and reputational consequences.

Strategic Misalignment

A flawed materiality assessment disconnects the sustainability programme from the company's actual business strategy. Sustainability should be integrated into strategic decision-making, risk management, and value creation. When the materiality assessment does not accurately reflect the company's true sustainability landscape, this integration becomes impossible.

5. The Right Approach: From External Input to Internal Ownership

The solution is not to abandon external data providers or consultants. They bring valuable sector knowledge, regulatory expertise, benchmarking data, and methodological rigour. The solution is to fundamentally change how their output is used.

The output from your data provider or consultant is an INPUT to your materiality process. It is not the process itself.

The external assessment should be treated as the first draft of an internal process, not the final version. It is a starting point for critical internal review, challenge, and refinement. The following framework outlines the key steps.

1
Internal Cross-Functional Review
  • Review the external assessment with a cross-functional team including operations, finance, risk, legal, procurement, and human resources
  • Examine each identified material topic against the team's operational knowledge
  • Ask: Does this topic genuinely reflect our specific operations, not just our sector in general?
  • Identify material topics that may be missing based on direct experience
  • Validate whether the prioritisation is correct given actual risk exposure and impact profile
  • Ask: Do we have the data and operational capability to meaningfully measure and manage this topic?
2
Stakeholder Validation
  • Test the reviewed assessment against genuine stakeholder input
  • Conduct targeted engagement with key stakeholder groups — not relying on proxy data from the external provider
  • Engage internal employees, major customers, critical suppliers, investors, and community representatives
  • Capture insights that no algorithm can replicate
3
Double Materiality Stress-Testing
  • For companies subject to CSRD or adopting double materiality voluntarily, stress-test each topic from both impact and financial materiality perspectives
  • Examine specific impact chains: how the company affects people and environment
  • Examine financial transmission mechanisms: how sustainability issues create financial risks and opportunities
  • Use company-specific rather than generic data
4
Strategic Alignment Check
  • Test the refined assessment against the company's business strategy, risk register, and forward-looking plans
  • Ensure material topics align with known strategic risks and opportunities
  • If the materiality assessment and the corporate strategy tell different stories about what matters, something is wrong
  • Resolve disconnects before implementation
5
Documentation and Governance
  • Thoroughly document the entire review process
  • Under CSRD and similar frameworks, the materiality assessment process is itself subject to assurance
  • Demonstrate not only what the material topics are, but how the conclusions were reached
  • Document what challenges were made, what changes resulted from internal review, and who approved the final assessment
  • Build a clear audit trail from external input to final approved assessment

7. The Regulatory Context

The urgency of this issue is amplified by the evolving regulatory landscape. Under the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), companies must conduct robust double materiality assessments, and those assessments are subject to third-party assurance. The European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) issued by EFRAG provide detailed guidance on what a compliant materiality assessment process looks like, including requirements for stakeholder engagement, documentation, and governance oversight.

Similarly, the ISSB standards under IFRS S1 and S2 require companies to identify and disclose sustainability-related risks and opportunities that are material to investors. While the ISSB focuses on financial materiality, the rigour expected in the identification process is substantial.

In both cases, regulators and assurance providers will look beyond the output of the materiality assessment to examine the process. A company that simply presents an externally generated document without evidence of internal review, challenge, and refinement will face increasingly difficult questions. The EU's new regulation on ESG rating providers, taking effect in July 2026, further underscores the scrutiny being applied to the entire ESG data ecosystem.

8. Recommendations for Sustainability Teams

Based on our experience, the following actions are relevant for sustainability and ESG teams:

Reframe the Relationship with Your Data Provider

View their output as a valuable input, not a finished product. Set expectations internally that the external assessment will require review and refinement before implementation.

Establish a Formal Internal Review Process

Create a cross-functional review committee with clear terms of reference. Document the review process, including what was challenged, what was changed, and why.

Invest in Genuine Stakeholder Engagement

Do not rely solely on proxy data. Engage directly with your most important stakeholder groups to validate and refine the assessment.

Stress-Test for Double Materiality

Ensure that both impact and financial materiality are assessed with company-specific rigour, not just sector-level assumptions.

Align with Strategy

Cross-reference the materiality assessment against your corporate strategy, risk register, and forward-looking business plans. Resolve any disconnects before implementation.

Prepare for Assurance

Build the documentation trail from day one. Assurance providers will want to see evidence of a robust, internally owned process, not just an externally purchased document.

Review and Refresh Regularly

Materiality is not static. As your business evolves, so will your material topics. Build annual review cycles into your governance processes.

9. Conclusion

The growing availability of data-driven materiality assessment tools represents a genuine advance for the sustainability profession. These platforms provide valuable sector intelligence, regulatory mapping, and benchmarking data that would be difficult and expensive for individual companies to replicate.

However, accessibility must not be confused with accuracy. A materiality assessment is a company-specific judgement about what sustainability topics are most relevant and important to that particular organisation. No external provider, regardless of how sophisticated their platform, can make that judgement with the same depth of understanding as the company itself.

Companies that take an externally produced materiality assessment and implement it without question are building their sustainability programmes on a foundation that is roughly 75% correct. That may be a reasonable starting point, but it is not an acceptable end point.

The remaining 25% represents real risk: wasted resources, regulatory exposure, assurance difficulties, and a sustainability programme that does not reflect what actually matters.

The path forward is clear. Use the external assessment as the high-quality input it is. Then apply your own knowledge, engage your stakeholders, challenge the assumptions, and take ownership of the result. The materiality assessment that emerges from this process will be more accurate, more defensible, and more strategically valuable than anything an algorithm alone can produce.

ISO 37001 Compliance Checklist

1

Develop a roadmap for the implementation of an ABMS and eventual certification

What Auditors Look For

Auditors will examine whether your organisation has conducted a thorough baseline assessment before implementation. They expect to see evidence of gap analysis and a realistic implementation timeline.

Red flag: Organisations that cannot demonstrate awareness of past compliance issues or industry enforcement trends may lack the contextual understanding needed for effective risk assessment.
Why this matters: A documented roadmap demonstrates intentionality and helps auditors understand your organisation's compliance journey and commitment level.
2

Identify the potential scope of the ABMS and conduct a risk assessment

What Auditors Look For

Risk assessments must be comprehensive and specific to your organisation. Auditors expect to see documented consideration of industry, geography, business model, and third-party relationships.

Red flag: Generic risk assessments that don't reference specific jurisdictions, transaction types, or business partner categories. Risk scores without documented rationale or quantitative analysis.
Why this matters: The risk assessment forms the foundation for all subsequent controls. Auditors use it to evaluate whether your ABMS is proportionate to your actual risk exposure.
3

Develop an anti-bribery policy

What Auditors Look For

Your anti-bribery policy must explicitly prohibit all forms of bribery, including facilitation payments, and apply to all personnel and business associates. Auditors review policy language for clarity and completeness.

Red flag: Policies that use vague language like "inappropriate payments" without defining bribery, or policies that only apply to certain employee levels or business units.
Why this matters: The policy is the cornerstone document auditors review first. Ambiguous policies create enforcement gaps and suggest inadequate commitment to anti-bribery compliance.
4

Seek leadership commitment from the governing body and top management

What Auditors Look For

Auditors look for documented evidence of board-level engagement with anti-bribery matters, including meeting minutes, policy approvals, and regular reporting to governance bodies.

Red flag: No evidence of board-level oversight or approval. Policies signed only at operational levels without C-suite or board endorsement. Absence of anti-bribery topics in board meeting agendas.
Why this matters: Tone from the top is critical. Auditors assess whether leadership commitment is genuine by examining how anti-bribery matters are prioritised at the highest levels.
5

Establish an anti-bribery compliance function

What Auditors Look For

The compliance function must have demonstrable independence from operational management. Auditors examine reporting lines, budget allocation, and decision-making authority.

Red flag: Compliance function reports to commercial leadership rather than the board or audit committee. No dedicated budget or headcount for anti-bribery activities. Compliance officer lacks authority to halt suspicious transactions.
Why this matters: An under-resourced or compromised compliance function cannot effectively enforce the ABMS. Auditors evaluate whether the function can operate without undue influence.
6

Develop an annual plan for the ABMS

What Auditors Look For

Auditors expect to see a documented annual plan with measurable objectives, assigned responsibilities, timelines, and allocated resources for each ABMS initiative.

Red flag: No documented annual plan. Objectives that are vague or unmeasurable. Plans that haven't been updated to reflect changes in risk profile or organisational structure.
Why this matters: An annual plan demonstrates that anti-bribery compliance is an ongoing priority, not a one-time exercise. It provides the roadmap auditors use to assess progress.
7

Awareness, training and communication

What Auditors Look For

Training programmes must be tailored to risk exposure levels. Auditors review training records for completion rates, comprehension testing, and coverage of high-risk personnel and business associates.

Red flag: Generic training without role-based customisation. Training records without completion dates or test scores. High-risk personnel (sales, procurement, agents) not receiving enhanced training.
Why this matters: Effective training builds a culture of compliance. Auditors assess whether personnel understand their obligations and can recognise bribery risks in their daily work.
8

Create a documentation system for the ABMS

What Auditors Look For

Auditors require a clear audit trail for all ABMS activities. Documentation must be organised, accessible, and demonstrate consistent record-keeping practices across the organisation.

Red flag: Fragmented documentation across multiple systems. Missing acknowledgment signatures for policy receipt. Due diligence files without documented decision rationale or approval dates.
Why this matters: Documentation is the evidence auditors rely on to verify compliance. Incomplete or inconsistent records suggest control gaps and make it difficult to demonstrate ABMS effectiveness.
9

Introduce the ABMS into the employment process

What Auditors Look For

Anti-bribery requirements must be embedded into HR processes from recruitment onwards. Auditors examine employment contracts, onboarding procedures, and background screening practices.

Red flag: Employment contracts without anti-bribery clauses. No background screening for high-risk positions. Onboarding that doesn't include anti-bribery training acknowledgment.
Why this matters: Integrating anti-bribery into employment ensures all personnel understand expectations from day one. It also provides legal basis for disciplinary action.
10

Implement a due diligence programme to detect and prevent bribery risk

What Auditors Look For

Third-party due diligence must be risk-based and documented. Auditors expect to see screening criteria, approval workflows, and ongoing monitoring for high-risk relationships.

Red flag: Due diligence files without documented business rationale. Agent appointments without enhanced screening. No periodic re-screening of existing high-risk third parties.
Why this matters: Third parties represent one of the highest bribery risks. Auditors assess whether your due diligence is proportionate to the risk each relationship presents.
11

Implement financial controls to manage bribery risk

What Auditors Look For

Financial controls must include segregation of duties, appropriate approval thresholds, and regular review of high-risk transactions. Auditors examine payment processes and expense management.

Red flag: No segregation between those approving payments and those recording them. Cash payments in high-risk jurisdictions without enhanced approval. Unusual commission structures (success fees over 15%, payments to third countries).
Why this matters: Financial controls are a critical defense against bribery. Auditors look for evidence that payments are properly authorised and traceable.
12

Implement non-financial controls to manage bribery risk

What Auditors Look For

Non-financial controls address conflict of interest, procurement integrity, and related misconduct. Auditors review procurement processes, COI registers, and related policy enforcement.

Red flag: No competitive bidding requirements for significant purchases. Conflicts of interest register not maintained or updated. No process for declaring personal interests in business decisions.
Why this matters: Non-financial controls prevent conditions that enable bribery. Auditors assess whether your organisation has addressed the full spectrum of corruption risks.
13

Require controlled organisations and business associates to implement anti-bribery controls

What Auditors Look For

Auditors examine whether anti-bribery requirements flow down to controlled entities and business partners through contracts, policies, and ongoing monitoring.

Red flag: Contracts without anti-bribery warranties or termination rights. Subsidiaries operating without documented anti-bribery controls. No audit rights over high-risk business associates.
Why this matters: Your organisation can be liable for the actions of third parties. Auditors verify that you have appropriate controls and contractual protections in place.
14

Establish a process for gifts, travel and entertainment (GTE) and donations/sponsorships

What Auditors Look For

GTE and donation processes must have clear thresholds, approval workflows, and documentation requirements. Auditors review registers and analyse spending patterns.

Red flag: Thresholds set without reference to local law or industry norms. Hospitality spend that spikes before major contract decisions. No pre-approval for government official entertainment.
Why this matters: GTE and donations are common bribery vehicles. Well-documented processes with appropriate thresholds demonstrate mature anti-bribery controls.
15

Implement a reporting system to raise concerns related to bribery

What Auditors Look For

Speak-up channels must be accessible, confidential, and trusted. Auditors review channel usage metrics, case handling procedures, and evidence of non-retaliation enforcement.

Red flag: Whistleblowing channels that report to operational management rather than compliance or the board. Zero reports received (suggests lack of trust or awareness). No documented case outcomes.
Why this matters: An effective reporting system is essential for detecting bribery. Auditors assess whether the channel is genuinely accessible and whether concerns are properly investigated.
16

Implement an investigations policy and procedure

What Auditors Look For

Investigation procedures must be documented, consistent, and include appropriate safeguards. Auditors review investigation files for process compliance and appropriate escalation.

Red flag: No documented investigation procedure. Investigations conducted by personnel with potential conflicts. No evidence of case tracking or outcome documentation.
Why this matters: Proper investigation procedures ensure concerns are addressed fairly and consistently. Auditors verify that your organisation can respond appropriately to bribery allegations.
17

Establish a monitoring programme to review ABMS effectiveness

What Auditors Look For

Monitoring must be systematic and documented. Auditors expect to see internal audit coverage, management reviews, and evidence that findings are reported to appropriate governance bodies.

Red flag: No evidence of periodic control testing or internal audit coverage of anti-bribery controls. Monitoring findings not reported to the board or audit committee. No documented management review.
Why this matters: Monitoring demonstrates that the ABMS is actively managed. Auditors look for evidence that control weaknesses are identified and addressed proactively.
18

Remediate non-conformities and continually improve the ABMS

What Auditors Look For

Continuous improvement must be demonstrable through documented remediation of findings, root cause analysis, and evidence of ABMS enhancements over time.

Red flag: Repeat audit findings without evidence of remediation. No root cause analysis for identified issues. ABMS unchanged despite organisational or risk profile changes.
Why this matters: Certification auditors look for evidence that your ABMS evolves in response to lessons learned. Stagnant programmes suggest compliance is treated as a one-time exercise.

What Auditors Look For: ISO 37001 Red Flags

When conducting ISO 37001 assurance engagements, auditors commonly flag the following areas as concerns. Understanding these red flags helps ensure your anti-bribery management system meets verification standards.

Documentation Red Flags
  • No evidence of board-level oversight or approval of the anti-bribery policy
  • Risk assessments that lack quantitative analysis or jurisdictional considerations
  • Training records without evidence of completion or comprehension testing
  • Third-party due diligence files without documented decision rationale
Control Design Red Flags
  • Gift/entertainment thresholds set without reference to local law or industry norms
  • No segregation between those approving payments and those recording them
  • Whistleblowing channels that report to operational management, not compliance
  • No evidence of periodic control testing or internal audit coverage
Operational Red Flags
  • Cash payments in high-risk jurisdictions without enhanced approval
  • Unusual commission structures (success fees over 15%, payments to third countries)
  • Agent appointments without documented business rationale
  • Hospitality spend patterns that spike before major contract decisions

ISO 37008 Internal Investigations Management Checklist

ISO 37008 provides guidance on establishing and managing internal investigation processes within organisations. Effective investigations are critical for addressing misconduct, protecting organisational integrity, and ensuring compliance with legal and regulatory requirements.

About This Checklist

This checklist covers the key elements of ISO 37008 for managing internal investigations. It addresses investigation planning, evidence handling, interviewing techniques, documentation, and legal considerations to help organisations conduct thorough, fair, and legally defensible investigations.

Related Standards: ISO 37002 Whistleblowing (investigations often follow whistleblower reports) ISO 37001 Anti-Bribery (bribery allegations require investigation)
1

Establish the investigations management framework

What Auditors Look For

Auditors assess whether the investigations function has genuine independence from business operations. They examine board-level oversight, dedicated budget allocation, and whether the function can investigate senior management without interference.

Investigations function reports solely to operational management with no audit committee oversight or independent escalation path.
Independence ensures investigations can pursue misconduct at any level without pressure to suppress findings or limit scope.
2

Define investigation triggers and thresholds

What Auditors Look For

Auditors verify that triage decisions are documented consistently. They look for clear criteria defining when allegations warrant formal investigation versus informal resolution, and evidence that similar matters receive similar treatment.

No documented rationale for declining to investigate allegations, or pattern of closing cases involving senior personnel without investigation.
Consistent triage criteria prevent accusations of selective enforcement and demonstrate the organisation takes all allegations seriously regardless of the subject's position.
3

Plan the investigation approach

What Auditors Look For

Auditors examine investigation plans for scope clarity, investigator qualifications, and conflict-of-interest assessments. They verify that plans identify all potential evidence sources and establish realistic timelines with appropriate resource allocation.

Investigator assigned has direct reporting relationship to investigation subject, or no documented conflict assessment before assignment.
Proper planning ensures investigations are thorough, fair, and legally defensible while avoiding scope creep or premature conclusions.
4

Secure and preserve evidence

What Auditors Look For

Auditors scrutinise chain of custody documentation, legal hold processes, and evidence storage security. They verify that digital evidence was forensically preserved and that physical evidence handling follows documented protocols.

Evidence collected without chain of custody documentation, or digital data accessed without forensic imaging that could alter metadata.
Proper evidence preservation ensures findings are admissible in legal proceedings and cannot be challenged on procedural grounds.
5

Conduct interviews and gather information

What Auditors Look For

Auditors review interview documentation for procedural fairness, including whether interviewees were informed of their rights, offered legal representation where appropriate, and given opportunity to review and correct their statements.

Interview records lack signatures or acknowledgments, or show leading questions that could suggest coercion or bias.
Fair interview processes protect both witness rights and the integrity of evidence, reducing risk of successful legal challenges to findings.
6

Analyse findings and draw conclusions

What Auditors Look For

Auditors evaluate whether conclusions logically follow from documented evidence. They examine credibility assessments, the standard of proof applied, and whether alternative explanations were properly considered and ruled out.

Conclusions drawn without documented analysis of contradicting evidence, or findings that rely on single-source testimony without corroboration.
Rigorous analysis ensures findings withstand scrutiny and provides defensible basis for disciplinary or legal action.
7

Document and report investigation outcomes

What Auditors Look For

Auditors assess report quality for clarity, objectivity, and evidentiary support. They verify that distribution was appropriately restricted and that the report clearly distinguishes between facts, analysis, and recommendations.

Investigation reports that omit methodology description, fail to address scope limitations, or do not clearly link findings to supporting evidence.
Well-structured reports enable informed decision-making by leadership and demonstrate thorough, professional investigation conduct.
8

Address legal and compliance considerations

What Auditors Look For

Auditors verify compliance with employment law, data protection regulations, and privilege protocols. They examine whether legal counsel was appropriately engaged and whether self-reporting obligations were identified and addressed.

Cross-border data transfers during investigation without legal review, or failure to identify mandatory regulatory reporting triggers.
Legal compliance protects the organisation from liability exposure and ensures investigation outcomes can be acted upon without procedural challenges.
9

Close investigations and implement remediation

What Auditors Look For

Auditors examine closure procedures for completeness, including whether all investigation steps were finished, disciplinary actions were proportionate and consistent, and systemic issues identified during investigation were addressed through remediation.

Pattern of investigations closing without documented outcomes, or disciplinary actions inconsistent with severity of substantiated findings.
Proper closure demonstrates investigations achieve their purpose of addressing misconduct and strengthening controls, not merely documenting problems.
10

Monitor, review and improve the investigations system

What Auditors Look For

Auditors assess whether the organisation tracks meaningful KPIs, conducts quality reviews of closed investigations, and uses trend analysis to identify systemic issues. They verify that lessons learned feed back into policy and procedure updates.

No metrics tracked beyond basic case counts, or recurring investigation themes without corresponding control improvements.
Continuous improvement demonstrates mature governance and maximises the value of investigation findings for organisational learning.

Red Flags in Internal Investigations

!

Evidence disappearing or being altered after an investigation is announced

!

Witnesses changing their accounts or refusing to cooperate

!

Senior management attempting to influence investigation outcomes

!

Retaliation against whistleblowers or witnesses during the investigation

!

Conflicts of interest among assigned investigators

!

Inadequate documentation or breaks in chain of custody

!

Pressure to close investigations prematurely

!

Pattern of similar allegations that are repeatedly unfounded

Self-Assessment Questions

Does your organisation have a documented investigation policy that is regularly reviewed and updated?

Are investigators properly trained and do they have access to appropriate resources and expertise?

Is there a clear escalation path for serious allegations, including those involving senior management?

How do you ensure evidence is preserved and chain of custody is maintained?

Are investigation outcomes consistently followed through with appropriate remediation and disciplinary action?

Do you track and report on investigation metrics to identify trends and areas for improvement?

What Auditors Look For: ISO 37008 Investigations Red Flags

When assessing investigation processes under ISO 37008, auditors commonly flag the following process and documentation concerns.

Process Red Flags
  • Investigation scope not documented at commencement
  • No protocols for evidence preservation and chain of custody
  • Investigator reports to subject of investigation
Documentation Red Flags
  • Interview notes not contemporaneously recorded
  • No documented criteria for case escalation or closure
  • Root cause analysis not documented

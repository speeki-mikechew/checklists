CSO's Anti-Greenwashing Checklist

Ten essential actions every chief sustainability officer should take to reduce the risk of greenwashing in ESG reporting and communications.

1

Establish comprehensive data boundaries

What Auditors Look For: Data Boundaries

Regulators and assurance providers scrutinise whether reporting boundaries are consistent with organisational boundaries used in financial reporting. They verify that material operations, subsidiaries, and supply chain impacts are not selectively excluded to present a more favourable environmental profile.

Red flag: Excluding high-emission subsidiaries or operations without documented rationale, or frequently changing boundaries in ways that make year-over-year comparisons impossible.
Under the EU Green Claims Directive and FTC Green Guides, environmental claims must cover the entire product lifecycle or clearly state their scope limitations.
2

Implement rigorous data verification

What Auditors Look For: Data Verification

Assurance providers evaluate whether verification covers material claims and whether verifiers possess appropriate competency. They examine the scope of assurance (limited vs. reasonable), the verifier's independence, and whether findings were addressed before publication.

Red flag: Self-verification or using verifiers with financial relationships, limited assurance presented as comprehensive validation, or verification scope that excludes the most material environmental claims.
ISAE 3000 and AA1000AS require verifiers to be independent and competent. Regulators increasingly expect third-party verification for headline environmental claims.
3

Use absolute metrics alongside relative improvements

What Auditors Look For: Absolute vs. Relative Metrics

Auditors assess whether efficiency improvements (intensity metrics) are accompanied by absolute impact data. A company may reduce emissions per unit while total emissions increase substantially due to growth. Regulators view this as a common greenwashing pattern.

Red flag: Prominently reporting percentage reductions without absolute figures, cherry-picking favourable baseline years, or using non-standard intensity denominators that inflate apparent progress.
The UK Competition and Markets Authority's Green Claims Code requires that absolute environmental impacts be disclosed alongside efficiency metrics to avoid misleading consumers.
4

Adopt standardised frameworks and definitions

What Auditors Look For: Standardised Frameworks

Regulators examine whether environmental claims use recognised definitions and methodologies. Terms like 'carbon neutral', 'net zero', or 'sustainable' require precise criteria. Claims must align with established frameworks (GHG Protocol, ISO 14064, SBTi) rather than company-invented definitions.

Red flag: Using terms like 'eco-friendly' or 'green' without defined criteria, claiming 'carbon neutral' without disclosing offset quality standards, or creating proprietary sustainability definitions.
The EU Green Claims Directive mandates that environmental claims be substantiated using recognised methodologies and validated by accredited third parties.
5

Present balanced performance narratives

What Auditors Look For: Balanced Narratives

Assurance providers assess whether sustainability communications present a balanced view. They look for disclosure of missed targets, areas of poor performance, and challenges alongside achievements. Cherry-picking positive results while omitting negative trends is a hallmark of greenwashing.

Red flag: Omitting missed targets from public reports, using timeframes that maximise apparent improvement, or burying negative performance data in appendices while highlighting successes in executive summaries.
CSRD double materiality requirements mandate disclosure of negative impacts. Selective disclosure can constitute misleading conduct under consumer protection regulations.

6

Ensure claims match resource allocation

What Auditors Look For: Resource Alignment

Regulators examine whether environmental commitments are backed by proportionate investment, staffing, and operational changes. The gap between stated sustainability priorities and actual resource allocation is a key indicator of greenwashing risk.

Red flag: Claiming climate leadership while sustainability budget represents less than 1% of total expenditure, or making net-zero commitments without capital allocation plans in financial disclosures.
Investors increasingly cross-reference sustainability claims with capex disclosures and executive compensation structures. Inconsistency suggests 'cheap talk' greenwashing.
7

Validate third-party certifications and awards

What Auditors Look For: Certification Validity

Assurance providers verify that certifications, eco-labels, and awards are current, from credible bodies, and apply to the claims being made. They assess whether product-level certifications are being improperly extended to organisation-wide claims.

Red flag: Displaying expired certifications, using self-created eco-labels, or claiming sustainability awards without disclosing that participation was paid or selection criteria were minimal.
The ISO 14024 standard defines requirements for credible eco-labels. Regulators view misuse of certifications as a serious form of greenwashing warranting enforcement action.
8

Ground future commitments in present action

What Auditors Look For: Future Commitments

Regulators scrutinise the credibility of forward-looking environmental claims. Long-term commitments (e.g., 2050 net-zero pledges) must be supported by interim milestones, transition plans, and evidence of current action. Aspirational language without substantiation is increasingly challenged.

Red flag: Making 2050 commitments without 2030 targets, announcing net-zero goals without published transition plans, or emphasising distant aspirations while current emissions continue rising.
The UK Advertising Standards Authority has ruled against companies making net-zero claims without credible interim targets and transition plans.

Red Flags to Avoid

1 Reporting only relative improvements without absolute impact data
2 Using undefined terms like 'carbon neutral' or 'sustainable' without criteria
3 Emphasising future goals while minimising current performance gaps
4 Claiming organisation-wide benefits from limited initiatives
5 Changing baselines or methodologies without clear explanation

Quick Self-Assessment Questions

Before publishing any sustainability communication, ask:

1 Can we provide specific data to support every environmental claim?
2 Have we included context that might change how stakeholders interpret our progress?
3 Would our competitors, regulators or NGOs reach the same conclusions from this data?
4 Have we presented both our strong performance and our challenges openly?
5 Do our environmental claims match our actual business priorities and investments?

What Auditors Look For: ESG Data Verification Red Flags

When providing assurance over ESG disclosures, auditors commonly flag the following data management, governance, and disclosure concerns.

Data Management Red Flags
  • Manual data aggregation from multiple disconnected sources
  • No defined data ownership or accountability structure
  • KPIs calculated differently across business units
  • Historical data restated without documented rationale
Governance Red Flags
  • ESG metrics not subject to same controls as financial reporting
  • Materiality assessment conducted without stakeholder input documentation
  • Targets set without documented baseline methodology
  • No internal audit or second-line review of ESG disclosures
Disclosure Red Flags
  • Forward-looking statements without stated assumptions
  • Peer comparisons using non-standardised metrics
  • Environmental claims not tied to verified programmes
  • Social metrics lacking demographic or geographic disaggregation

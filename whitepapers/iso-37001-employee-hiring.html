Clause 7.2.2.2 of ISO 37001:2025 is one of the most consistently misunderstood requirements in the entire standard. It imposes three distinct obligations for all positions exposed to more than a low bribery risk. Most organisations address only one, and even that one is usually done inadequately. The clause requires: (a) due diligence before employment, transfer, or promotion; (b) periodic review of performance incentives; (c) periodic compliance declarations from in-scope personnel, top management, and the governing body.

This whitepaper examines each sub-clause in detail, explains why common practice falls short, and provides a practical implementation framework for organisations seeking genuine compliance — not just a documented process that satisfies the letter of the requirement while missing its substance entirely.

1. What the Standard Actually Requires

Clause 7.2.2.2 of ISO 37001:2025 contains three distinct mandatory sub-clauses, each addressing a different aspect of how organisations manage the human risk dimension of bribery. These are not alternatives or a menu from which an organisation may select. All three apply.

Sub-Clause (a) — Due Diligence Before Appointment: The organisation shall undertake due diligence prior to employment, and where practicable prior to redeployment or promotion, of personnel in positions that are exposed to more than a low bribery risk. This due diligence involves two tests, both of which must be satisfied: first, whether it is appropriate to employ or redeploy the person; and second, whether there are reasonable grounds to believe that the person will comply with the anti-bribery management system (ABMS).

Sub-Clause (b) — Periodic Review of Performance Incentives: The organisation shall periodically review the performance bonuses, targets, and other elements that incentivise personnel in positions that are exposed to more than a low bribery risk, and shall take action to verify that these do not create incentives for bribery, and that adequate safeguards exist.

Sub-Clause (c) — Periodic Compliance Declarations: The organisation shall periodically obtain from in-scope personnel, top management, and the governing body a declaration confirming compliance with the ABMS, proportionate to the bribery risk to which they are exposed.

Clause 7.2.2.2 imposes three obligations, not one. An organisation that has addressed only due diligence — even if it has addressed it well — is meeting one-third of the requirement.

The remainder of this whitepaper addresses each sub-clause in turn, beginning with the one that is most commonly attempted yet most commonly done wrong.

2. Scope: Who Is In Scope?

Before examining each sub-clause, it is necessary to be precise about who falls within the scope of Clause 7.2.2.2. The standard links scope directly to the bribery risk assessment conducted under Clause 4.5. The obligation applies to all positions exposed to more than a low bribery risk as determined by that assessment.

This is not a fixed or universal list. Scope depends on the nature of the organisation, its sector, its geographic footprint, and the outputs of its Clause 4.5 risk assessment. However, in most large organisations the following categories will typically be in scope:

Sales and Commercial Functions

Particularly roles with direct interaction with government or state-owned enterprise customers, or roles operating in jurisdictions with elevated bribery risk ratings.

Procurement and Supply Chain

All roles with authority to select, onboard, approve, or pay suppliers, particularly where supplier relationships are not subject to competitive tendering.

Finance and Treasury

Roles with payment authority, particularly where payments may be made to third parties or agents in high-risk jurisdictions, or where discretionary spending approvals exist.

Government and Regulatory Affairs

All roles whose primary function involves interaction with public officials, regulatory bodies, or government agencies, regardless of jurisdiction.

Country Managers in High-Risk Jurisdictions

Senior leadership in countries rated high on the organisation’s bribery risk matrix, where local accountability structures may reduce visibility to group-level oversight.

Senior Leadership and Top Management

In addition to their functional risk exposure, top management and the governing body are separately referenced in Sub-Clause (c) for compliance declarations regardless of their operational risk rating.

The Anti-Bribery Compliance Function

ISO 37001 requires that the anti-bribery compliance officer (ABCO) function itself be subject to due diligence requirements, regardless of the risk rating assigned to the role through the standard risk assessment process.

The documented basis for the scope determination must be traceable to the Clause 4.5 risk assessment. An organisation that cannot demonstrate how its scope list was derived from a documented risk assessment will have difficulty satisfying an auditor on this point.

3. Sub-Clause (a): The Due Diligence Problem

Sub-Clause (a) is the provision most organisations have at least attempted to address. It is also the provision that is most systematically misunderstood. The core misunderstanding is the conflation of verification with investigation, and of screening with due diligence.

The Three Misconceptions

In assessing ABMS compliance across many organisations, three pervasive misconceptions appear repeatedly. Each represents a genuine attempt to address the sub-clause requirement; each falls short.

Misconception 1: Reference Checks Are Due Diligence

Reference checks are candidate-nominated, rarely independent, and not designed to elicit integrity information. A former manager confirming that an individual was “hardworking and a team player” provides no evidence whatsoever about the candidate’s propensity to comply with an ABMS. Reference checks are not due diligence under Clause 7.2.2.2 — they are part of a recruitment process.

Misconception 2: Sanctions and PEP Screening Is Due Diligence

Sanctions screening, politically exposed person (PEP) checks, and anti-money laundering (AML) database searches are binary list checks. A clean result tells you only that the individual does not appear on a specified watchlist at a specific point in time. It establishes nothing about their past conduct, their relationships, or their likelihood of complying with an ABMS. A clean sanctions result does not satisfy the “reasonable grounds to believe” test in Sub-Clause (a).

Misconception 3: Commercial Background Check Services Are Due Diligence

Background check services are verification tools. They confirm whether stated facts — employment history, academic qualifications, professional licences, criminal record — are accurate. They do not investigate bribery risk. They rely primarily on information provided by or about the candidate, and they do not assess whether a candidate is likely to comply with an anti-bribery management system. For low-risk roles, this may be proportionate. For high-risk roles, it is insufficient on its own.

The Due Diligence Comparison

Screening Type Primary Purpose Addresses Sub-Clause (a) Test?
Reference Checks Verify employment history and character from candidate-nominated sources No — not independent; candidate controls the information
Sanctions / PEP / AML Screening Identify individuals on regulatory watchlists No — binary check; a clean result does not establish reasonable belief in ABMS compliance
Commercial Background Checks Verify identity, criminal record, education, and stated employment history Partially — confirms factual accuracy, but not compliance propensity; insufficient for high-risk roles
Anti-Bribery Due Diligence (Clause 8.2 standard) Ascertain appropriateness of appointment and establish reasonable belief in ABMS compliance Yes — investigative, risk-proportionate, draws on independent sources

4. The Standard’s Guidance: Annex A.8.1

ISO 37001:2025 Annex A.8.1 provides normative guidance on the seven dimensions that due diligence should address for personnel. These dimensions are not merely illustrative suggestions — they represent the standard’s own specification of what a due diligence programme must be capable of addressing. Most organisations address two or three. Very few address all seven.

(a) Discussing Anti-Bribery Policy at Interview

The organisation should discuss its anti-bribery policy with candidates during the interview process and document the candidate’s response. This is a qualitative judgement dimension: how does the candidate respond when asked about bribery risk, ethical dilemmas, or situations where they faced pressure to act improperly? Most organisations never ask these questions. Fewer still document the response and treat it as part of the due diligence record.

(b) Verifying Qualifications

Verification of academic qualifications, professional licences, and certifications from independent sources — not from documents provided by the candidate. This is the one dimension most organisations handle adequately, usually through their standard background check process.

(c) Obtaining Satisfactory References

The emphasis in this dimension falls on the word “satisfactory” — it is a qualitative assessment, not simply the act of collecting a reference. References that do not address the candidate’s integrity, ethical conduct, or behaviour in situations where there was potential for improper advantage are of limited value. References that are unsatisfactory in any respect must be escalated and assessed — not ignored or filed away.

(d) Determining Prior Involvement in Bribery

This goes significantly beyond a criminal record check. The inquiry should extend to adverse media searches, regulatory actions, debarment list searches, civil proceedings, and any other publicly available information about the candidate’s history. A candidate may have been at the centre of a corruption investigation that resulted in no criminal conviction — civil settlements, regulatory findings, or debarment from public contracts are all relevant. A criminal record check will not surface any of this.

(e) Verifying the Organisation Is NOT Offering Employment as a Reward

This is the most overlooked dimension in Annex A.8.1, and arguably the most conceptually important. It flips the lens: rather than asking whether the candidate poses a bribery risk, it asks whether the organisation’s offer of employment is itself an act of bribery — a reward for past corrupt conduct that benefited the organisation. The most common example is the hire of a former procurement official who consistently favoured the organisation in supplier selection decisions. If the true reason for the hire is to reward past conduct rather than because of the individual’s genuine merit, the hire may itself constitute bribery.

(f) Verifying the Purpose of the Employment Offer Is NOT to Secure Future Advantage

This dimension asks whether the true purpose of the hire is to leverage the individual’s relationships, access, or influence with government bodies or regulators to secure future improper advantage. The hire of a well-connected former regulator into a regulatory affairs role is not automatically problematic — but if the primary purpose of the hire is to exploit that access, and if the organisation has not examined that question, it may have failed both this dimension and its broader ABMS obligations.

(g) Identifying Relationships to Public Officials

Beyond PEP database checks, which identify only the individual themselves, this dimension requires inquiry into family relationships, business interests, and political connections that could create a conflict of interest or bribery risk in the performance of the role. A candidate who is not a PEP but whose spouse holds a senior position in a government procurement function may present significant risk in a sales or bid management role.

If your due diligence programme cannot tell you anything about a candidate that the candidate did not already tell you, it is not due diligence. It is verification.

5. What Genuine Due Diligence Looks Like

Genuine due diligence under Clause 7.2.2.2 is risk-proportionate. The standard does not require the same intensity of inquiry for a moderate-risk role as for a high-risk senior appointment. What it does require is that the depth of inquiry be calibrated to the risk and that the process be capable of addressing all seven dimensions of Annex A.8.1 — at least at a level proportionate to the risk profile of the role.

Moderate-Risk Roles

Moderate-Risk Due Diligence Package

Core Components

Identity and right-to-work verification; criminal record check where legally permitted; employment history verification from independent sources (not candidate-provided documents alone); education and professional qualification verification; sanctions, PEP, and AML screening; basic adverse media search; structured conflict of interest declaration from the candidate covering business interests, family relationships with customers and suppliers, and relationships with public officials.

High-Risk Roles

All components of the moderate-risk package apply, plus the following additional elements:

Enhanced Due Diligence Components

Additional Requirements for High-Risk Appointments

Enhanced media and intelligence analysis drawing on multiple independent sources; source-based inquiries using independent contacts in the candidate’s professional background — not candidate-nominated references; conflict of interest investigation covering directorships, business interests, financial interests, and family connections in the relevant market; jurisdictional risk overlay for candidates with career history in high-risk jurisdictions; structured integrity assessment through behavioural and scenario-based interview questions specifically designed to assess ethical reasoning; financial probity review where legally permissible; reverse lens assessment addressing Annex A.8.1(e) and (f) — whether the offer could constitute a reward or a mechanism for securing future advantage; public official relationship mapping addressing Annex A.8.1(g).

Transfers and Promotions

The clause explicitly requires that due diligence be conducted before redeployment or promotion into positions exposed to more than a low bribery risk, where practicable. This is not satisfied by the checks conducted at original hire. A person who joined the organisation in an administrative role and is now being promoted to a country manager position in a high-risk jurisdiction must be subject to due diligence appropriate to the new role — before the appointment takes effect.

This requirement is regularly missed. Organisations tend to treat internal appointments as exempt from due diligence requirements, reasoning that the person is already known. The standard does not support this reasoning.

Anonymised Audit Findings

The following examples illustrate common audit findings in this area. All are drawn from real assessments and have been anonymised.

Audit Finding — Major Nonconformity

Regional Director, Sub-Saharan Africa

Candidate subject to standard commercial background check. Clean result. No independent investigation conducted. No adverse media search. No conflict of interest assessment beyond self-declaration. Role involved primary responsibility for government sales in multiple jurisdictions rated high on the organisation’s risk matrix. The individual was implicated in a facilitation payment scheme within 18 months of appointment. A post-incident investigation found multiple publicly available indicators of prior integrity concerns that the background check had not surfaced.

Audit Finding — Minor Nonconformity

Chief Commercial Officer, Energy Sector

Standard verification process only. No investigative due diligence despite the role carrying responsibility for government-facing commercial activity across six jurisdictions. The candidate’s career history included a seven-year tenure with an entity that had been the subject of regulatory action in two jurisdictions — information that would have been surfaced by a competent adverse media search. The auditor raised a minor nonconformity on the grounds that the due diligence was not proportionate to the bribery risk of the appointment.

Audit Finding — Major Nonconformity

Internal Promotion to Head of Procurement

No due diligence conducted for internal promotion. The organisation’s position was that the individual had been with the organisation for eight years and was well known. Subsequent investigation triggered by a whistleblower disclosure revealed undisclosed business interests in two entities that were suppliers to the organisation, one of which had been approved during the individual’s tenure in a predecessor procurement role. The promotion due diligence requirement in Sub-Clause (a) had not been implemented.

Audit Finding — Minor Nonconformity

Country Manager Transfer, Southeast Asia

Individual transferred from a role that had been classified as moderate-risk to a country manager position in a jurisdiction rated high on the organisation’s bribery risk assessment. The only due diligence conducted was equivalent to the original moderate-risk hire checks. No enhanced investigation was undertaken. The auditor raised a minor nonconformity on the basis that the due diligence package was not calibrated to the risk level of the new appointment.

6. Sub-Clause (b): Performance Incentives

Sub-Clause (b) requires a periodic review of the performance bonuses, targets, and other incentivising elements applicable to in-scope personnel. The purpose is to verify that these structures do not, in practice, create incentives for bribery, and that adequate safeguards exist where incentive pressure is high.

This is the sub-clause most organisations have never addressed at all. It requires a structured review — not a one-off exercise — conducted jointly by the HR and compliance functions, of the incentive architecture applicable to the in-scope population.

What the Review Must Cover

Revenue and Sales Targets

Are targets calibrated to account for the additional difficulty and risk of operating in high-risk markets? A sales target that is identical for a representative operating in a low-risk domestic market and one operating in a jurisdiction with pervasive public sector corruption creates unequal pressure in contexts where the temptation to take improper shortcuts is materially different.

Bonus Structures

Do bonus structures include any compliance or governance component? Is there a mechanism for clawing back bonuses where improper conduct is subsequently discovered? A structure that rewards only revenue outcomes with no counterbalancing compliance element and no clawback mechanism is a structure that creates one-sided incentives.

Procurement KPIs

Do key performance indicators for procurement personnel include metrics related to supplier due diligence completion rates, procurement policy compliance, or conflict of interest disclosure? Or do they measure only cost savings and cycle time — metrics that create pressure to cut corners?

Commission Structures

Are commission rates disproportionately high for contracts in high-risk jurisdictions or for contracts awarded by government customers? Disproportionate commission creates disproportionate temptation.

Non-Financial Incentives

Are promotions, professional development opportunities, or public recognition linked to metrics that could indirectly encourage bribery? A culture that celebrates exceptional revenue performance without examining how results were achieved is a culture that implicitly tolerates improper conduct.

Common Audit Findings on Incentive Review

No Documented Review Has Ever Been Conducted

The most common finding. The organisation has no record of a structured review of incentive structures against bribery risk. The sub-clause has simply not been implemented.

Sales Targets Identical Across Risk Profiles

In-scope sales personnel operating in high-risk jurisdictions carry the same revenue targets as those in low-risk markets, with no adjustment, no additional safeguards, and no documented rationale.

No Compliance Component and No Clawback

Bonus structures for in-scope personnel are entirely financial in their metrics, contain no compliance-related element, and include no clawback mechanism. There is no documented consideration of whether this structure creates bribery incentives.

HR and Compliance Functions Never Jointly Reviewed the Framework

The incentive framework was designed entirely by HR and finance. The compliance function was not involved in its design and has never reviewed it for bribery risk. There is no documented collaboration between the two functions on this topic.

Organisation Argues It Is a “Business Decision” Outside ABMS Scope

The organisation’s position is that incentive structure design is a commercial and HR matter, not a compliance matter. This reasoning is inconsistent with the express requirement of Sub-Clause (b) and will not withstand audit scrutiny.

One point warrants emphasis: the standard does not require the organisation to change its incentive structures. It requires the organisation to review them and to verify that adequate safeguards exist. An organisation may conclude — after a documented, genuine review — that its existing incentive structures are appropriate. What it cannot do is never conduct that review.

7. Sub-Clause (c): Compliance Declarations

Sub-Clause (c) requires the organisation to obtain, periodically, a declaration from in-scope personnel, top management, and the governing body confirming compliance with the ABMS. The declaration must be proportionate to the bribery risk to which the individual is exposed.

The compliance declaration serves two distinct functions: it creates a documented accountability mechanism — an individual’s confirmation that they understand and are complying with the ABMS — and it provides a platform for disclosure of concerns or potential conflicts that the individual believes the organisation should be aware of.

What a Compliant Process Looks Like

Defined Scope

The declaration process must extend to the full in-scope population as defined by the Clause 4.5 risk assessment, plus top management and the governing body as expressly required by the sub-clause. Distributing declarations only to a subset of the in-scope population — or to all employees regardless of risk — is not compliant.

Proportionate Frequency

Annual declarations are typically appropriate for moderate-risk personnel. Senior leadership and those in high-risk roles may warrant more frequent declarations. The frequency must be documented and justified.

Substantive Content

The declaration must specifically reference the ABMS and the individual’s obligations under it. A declaration that asks the individual to confirm compliance with “all applicable company policies” does not satisfy Sub-Clause (c). The declaration should also include a mechanism for disclosing concerns, actual or potential conflicts of interest, or anything else that the individual believes is relevant to the organisation’s ABMS.

Collection and Tracking

The process must be managed — distribution, collection, completion rates, and follow-up for non-responders must all be documented. A declaration process that distributes forms and does not track whether they are returned is not a functioning declaration process.

Review and Escalation

Declarations submitted must actually be reviewed by a qualified person. Disclosures made in declarations must be assessed and, where warranted, escalated. A process that collects declarations and files them without review provides no compliance value.

Common Audit Findings on Compliance Declarations

No Declaration Process Exists

The most common finding. The organisation has no compliance declaration process. Sub-Clause (c) has not been implemented.

Generic Policy Compliance Language

The organisation has a declaration process, but it asks personnel to confirm compliance with “all company policies and procedures.” There is no specific reference to the ABMS, no description of what ABMS compliance entails, and no mechanism for disclosure. This does not satisfy Sub-Clause (c).

Distributed to All Employees Rather Than In-Scope Population

Declarations are sent to all employees as a blanket exercise. While this may have administrative advantages, it demonstrates that the organisation has not identified its in-scope population and has not applied a risk-proportionate approach. It may also dilute the significance of the declaration by framing it as a routine administrative exercise rather than a substantive accountability mechanism.

Completion Rates Not Tracked

Declarations are distributed but the organisation has no record of how many were returned, who has not responded, or what follow-up was taken. There is no functioning management process behind the declaration exercise.

Declarations Collected but Never Reviewed

The process distributes and collects declarations. No one reviews them. Disclosures made within declarations — conflicts of interest, concerns, observations — have gone unnoticed because the file was not opened after collection.

8. Implementation Framework

Building a compliant Clause 7.2.2.2 programme does not require the organisation to design an entirely new infrastructure. In most cases, the components already exist in some form. The task is to make them coherent, calibrate them to risk, and document them in a way that demonstrates genuine compliance across all three sub-clauses.

1
Phase 1 — Foundation (Immediate)
  • Step 1: Map roles to bribery risk using the outputs of the Clause 4.5 risk assessment. Produce a documented, approved list of in-scope roles categorised by risk level (moderate or high). This list is the foundation for all three sub-clauses.
  • Step 2: Define tiered due diligence requirements for each risk category. Document what the moderate-risk package includes and what the high-risk package includes, with explicit reference to each of the seven Annex A.8.1 dimensions.
  • Step 3: Identify independent and reliable sources for high-risk investigations — open-source intelligence tools, specialist due diligence providers, and structured interview frameworks for integrity assessment.
2
Phase 2 — Build Controls (Within 60 Days)
  • Step 4: Separate reference checking from due diligence in all process documentation. Update HR procedures to reflect the distinction between candidate verification and ABMS-specific due diligence inquiry.
  • Step 5: Implement a pre-appointment completion requirement. No appointment to an in-scope role should proceed until the documented due diligence for that risk tier has been completed and cleared.
  • Step 6: Define triggers for refreshed due diligence on transfers and promotions. Update the HR approval workflow to require compliance sign-off before any transfer or promotion into an in-scope role is confirmed.
  • Step 7: Conduct the first documented review of performance incentive structures against bribery risk for the in-scope population. Involve both HR and compliance functions. Document the review and any findings.
3
Phase 3 — Sustain and Mature (Within 120 Days)
  • Step 8: Design and implement the anti-bribery compliance declaration process. Define the scope (in-scope population, top management, governing body), frequency, content, collection mechanism, and review and escalation procedures.
  • Step 9: Train HR teams and hiring managers on tiered due diligence requirements, including the seven Annex A.8.1 dimensions, the distinction between verification and investigation, and the requirement for pre-appointment completion.
  • Step 10: Establish a periodic review cycle for incentive structures — at least annual — with documented outputs and a compliance sign-off confirming that the review has occurred and its conclusions.
  • Step 11: Integrate all three sub-clause processes into the management review agenda under Clause 9.3, so that the governing body receives periodic reporting on due diligence outcomes, incentive review findings, and declaration completion rates.

9. Conclusion

Clause 7.2.2.2 of ISO 37001:2025 addresses three dimensions of the human risk that organisations face in managing bribery: the integrity of the people they appoint to positions of risk; the design of the financial and non-financial incentive structures that govern those people’s behaviour; and the ongoing accountability mechanism through which those people confirm their compliance.

Most organisations, if they have addressed the clause at all, have addressed only the first dimension — and they have addressed it inadequately. Background checks and sanctions screening are not due diligence. They are verification. The standard requires investigation proportionate to risk, covering all seven dimensions of Annex A.8.1, conducted before the appointment is made.

Items (e) and (f) of Annex A.8.1 — the requirement to consider whether the hire itself could constitute a form of bribery, whether as a reward for past conduct or as a mechanism for securing future advantage — are almost universally ignored. They are also conceptually the most important, because they require the organisation to examine its own motivations, not merely the candidate’s history.

The incentive review requirement of Sub-Clause (b) and the compliance declaration requirement of Sub-Clause (c) are frequently absent entirely. Neither is administratively complex. Both are demonstrably required by the standard. Both are tested in ABMS certification audits.

An organisation that genuinely implements all three sub-clauses of Clause 7.2.2.2 — with depth proportionate to risk, documented processes, and evidence of operation — has built a compliance programme that is materially more resilient to the human risk of bribery than the vast majority of its peers.

ESG Software Must-Haves Checklist

Evaluate ESG software platforms on audit trail, data integrity, and reporting capabilities that external assurance providers require when assessing ESG data quality. Ensure your software selection supports external assurance requirements.

Data Collection and Management

What Auditors Look For: Data Collection

Assurance providers evaluate whether ESG software maintains complete data provenance. They look for systems that document the original source of every data point, track all transformations, and provide clear lineage from raw input to reported figures.

Red flag: Software allows direct database edits without logging, or data can be imported without source documentation attached.
Why it matters: Under ISAE 3000/3410 assurance standards, auditors must be able to trace any reported metric back to its source data and verify all calculation steps.
2

Emissions and Carbon Accounting

What Auditors Look For: Carbon Accounting

Auditors scrutinize emissions calculation methodologies for GHG Protocol compliance. They verify that emission factors are current, region-appropriate, and properly documented. The software should clearly show which calculation method was used for each emissions category.

Red flag: Software uses generic global emission factors when location-specific factors are available, or does not document the emission factor source and version.
Why it matters: Limited assurance of GHG statements requires auditors to assess whether appropriate emission factors and calculation methodologies were applied consistently.
3

Reporting Framework Alignment

What Auditors Look For: Framework Alignment

Assurance providers assess whether software correctly maps data to disclosure requirements. They look for systems that maintain clear relationships between raw data, calculated metrics, and specific framework requirements (GRI indicators, ESRS datapoints, SASB metrics).

Red flag: Software generates reports without showing which underlying data supports each disclosure, or uses outdated framework templates that do not reflect current requirements.
Why it matters: CSRD requires reasonable assurance by 2028, meaning auditors must verify that disclosures are complete and accurately represent the underlying data per ESRS requirements.
4

Integration Capabilities

What Auditors Look For: System Integrations

Auditors evaluate the reliability of automated data feeds from source systems. They look for integration logging, error handling, and reconciliation capabilities that ensure data transferred from ERP, HRIS, or other systems matches the original source.

Red flag: API integrations do not log sync history, failed imports are not flagged, or there is no ability to reconcile imported totals against source systems.
Why it matters: Automated data collection reduces manual entry errors, but auditors need evidence that integrations function correctly and data integrity is maintained throughout the transfer process.
5

Materiality Assessment and Strategy

What Auditors Look For: Materiality Assessment

Auditors review the documentation behind materiality decisions to ensure they are evidence-based. For double materiality under CSRD, software should capture both stakeholder input and impact/financial risk assessments with clear rationale for inclusion or exclusion of topics.

Red flag: Materiality assessments cannot be versioned or dated, stakeholder feedback is not traceable to specific inputs, or the scoring methodology is not documented.
Why it matters: ESRS requires companies to document their double materiality assessment process, and auditors must verify this process was conducted appropriately with proper stakeholder engagement.
6

Compliance and Assurance Readiness

What Auditors Look For: Audit Trail Quality

This is the most critical capability for assurance. Auditors need complete, immutable records of who entered data, when changes were made, what the original values were, and approval workflows. The audit trail should be tamper-proof and exportable.

Red flag: Administrators can delete audit logs, change timestamps can be modified, or the system does not capture the "before" state when data is edited.
Why it matters: ISAE 3000 requires auditors to assess internal controls over ESG data. Without comprehensive audit trails, auditors cannot provide assurance that data has not been manipulated.
7

Analytics and Insights

What Auditors Look For: Analytics Reliability

Auditors assess whether analytics and calculated metrics are reproducible and based on transparent methodologies. They look for systems where calculation logic is documented, configurable parameters are logged, and the same inputs always produce the same outputs.

Red flag: Dashboard figures do not reconcile with underlying reports, calculation methodologies are "black box" without documentation, or trend analyses use inconsistent time periods.
Why it matters: Published metrics must be reproducible. Auditors performing analytical procedures need to understand and verify how aggregated figures and trends were calculated.
8

User Experience and Accessibility

What Auditors Look For: Access Controls

Auditors evaluate role-based access controls as part of the internal control environment. They look for clear segregation of duties - data entry should be separate from approval, and only authorized users should be able to publish or finalize reports.

Red flag: All users have administrator access, no approval workflows exist for data changes, or user access reviews are not documented.
Why it matters: Strong access controls reduce the risk of unauthorized data manipulation. Auditors must assess whether appropriate controls exist to maintain data integrity throughout the reporting process.
9

Supply Chain and Third-Party Management

What Auditors Look For: Supply Chain Data

Scope 3 emissions and supply chain data face heightened scrutiny due to inherent estimation challenges. Auditors look for systems that clearly distinguish between primary supplier data, industry averages, and estimates, with documented assumptions for each category.

Red flag: Supplier-reported data cannot be traced to source documentation, estimation methodologies are not documented, or there is no differentiation between actual and estimated data.
Why it matters: CSRD requires value chain disclosures, and auditors must assess data quality and the appropriateness of estimation methodologies used for Scope 3 and supply chain metrics.
10

Vendor Support and Service

What Auditors Look For: Vendor Stability

Auditors consider vendor continuity risk as part of their assessment. A vendor that may not survive creates risk that historical data, audit trails, and reporting capabilities could become inaccessible - undermining multi-year comparability and assurance continuity.

Red flag: No data export capabilities, proprietary data formats with no documented schema, or vendor cannot demonstrate financial stability and customer retention.
Why it matters: ESG reporting requires year-over-year comparability. If a vendor fails and data cannot be migrated, organisations may lose the ability to demonstrate consistent reporting methodologies to auditors.

Red Flags to Watch For

Outdated Emissions Factors

Platform uses emission factors that are several years old or not region-specific, leading to inaccurate carbon accounting.

No Framework Updates

Vendor has not updated the platform to reflect recent changes in CSRD, ESRS, or IFRS S1/S2 requirements.

Limited Integration Options

Platform cannot connect to your existing ERP, HRIS, or other critical data systems, requiring extensive manual data entry.

Poor Audit Trail

Insufficient documentation of data sources, calculations, and changes, making external assurance difficult or impossible.

Vendor Lock-In

No data export capabilities or API access, making it difficult to migrate to another platform if needed.

Unresponsive Support

Slow or unclear responses during the sales process often indicate poor customer support after purchase.

Critical Questions to Ask Vendors

How frequently do you update emissions factors and regulatory frameworks?

Look for vendors who update quarterly or at least twice per year to ensure compliance with evolving standards.

Can you provide client references in our industry and size?

Speak with current customers to understand real-world implementation challenges and platform performance.

What is your data migration and onboarding process?

Understand the timeline, resources required, and support provided during implementation.

How do you handle assurance readiness and external audits?

Ensure the platform provides audit trails, data lineage, and reports that meet assurance provider requirements.

What is included in the base price versus additional modules?

Clarify pricing structure to avoid unexpected costs for essential features down the line.

What are your data ownership and export policies?

Confirm that you retain full ownership of your data and can export it in standard formats if needed.

What Auditors Look For: ESG Software Assurance-Readiness

When assessing ESG software for assurance-readiness, auditors flag systems lacking these critical capabilities. Understanding these requirements helps ensure your software can support external assurance engagements.

Audit Trail Red Flags
  • No user-level access logs
  • Data can be modified without change history
  • Calculations not transparent or auditable
  • No ability to "freeze" reporting periods
Data Integrity Red Flags
  • Manual overrides without approval workflow
  • No validation rules on data input
  • API integrations without error logging
  • No reconciliation features between source and aggregated data
Reporting Red Flags
  • Cannot regenerate historical reports exactly as published
  • No version control on report templates or methodologies
  • Methodology documentation stored outside the system
  • Limited/no GRI, SASB, or framework-specific tagging

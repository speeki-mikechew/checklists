Sustainability assurance is no longer an optional enhancement for leading organisations. Across major jurisdictions, mandatory assurance requirements are taking effect — and the organisations best positioned for these engagements are those that have understood what auditors actually evaluate, and prepared accordingly.

This whitepaper sets out the landscape of sustainability assurance: the regulatory context, the standards governing how engagements are conducted, and the areas that auditors typically examine. It is written from an educational perspective — intended to help organisations understand what the assurance process involves, not to provide specific advice on any particular engagement.

1. Introduction: Why Sustainability Assurance Is Becoming Mandatory

For much of the past two decades, sustainability reporting was a voluntary exercise. Organisations published reports aligned to GRI, CDP, or TCFD frameworks on their own initiative, with no external verification required by law. The credibility of those reports rested largely on the organisation's own assertions about its data and methodology.

That era is ending. A convergence of regulatory developments across the European Union, the United Kingdom, and Asia-Pacific is making sustainability assurance mandatory — and the requirements are only becoming more stringent over time.

Three developments stand out as the most significant drivers of this shift:

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

The EU's CSRD mandates that large companies and listed SMEs have their sustainability reporting independently assured. For large companies, the requirement for limited assurance applies from financial years commencing on or after 1 January 2025, with a phased pathway towards reasonable assurance in subsequent years.

ISSA 5000 — International Standard on Sustainability Assurance

The International Auditing and Assurance Standards Board (IAASB) finalised ISSA 5000 in 2024 — the first dedicated global standard for sustainability assurance engagements. It provides a comprehensive framework governing how auditors plan, conduct, and report on sustainability assurance, applicable to both limited and reasonable assurance.

ISAE 3000 (Revised) — The Broader Framework

ISAE 3000 (Revised) governs assurance engagements on subject matter other than historical financial information. Many assurance providers have conducted sustainability engagements under ISAE 3000 for years; ISSA 5000 builds on this foundation with sustainability-specific requirements.

The direction of regulatory travel is clear and consistent across major economies. Organisations operating in the EU, UK, or globally through listed entities can expect sustainability assurance to become a standing regulatory requirement — not a one-time exercise.

2. What Is Sustainability Assurance?

Sustainability assurance is an independent evaluation of sustainability information — typically disclosed in a sustainability report or integrated report — conducted by a qualified, accredited third party. The assurance provider evaluates whether the sustainability information presented by the organisation is free from material misstatement and prepared in accordance with applicable reporting standards.

2.1 Limited vs Reasonable Assurance

Assurance engagements operate across two levels of rigour, and the distinction matters significantly for how organisations prepare:

Aspect Limited Assurance Reasonable Assurance
Conclusion language "Nothing has come to our attention that causes us to believe…" "In our opinion, the sustainability information is prepared, in all material respects, in accordance with…"
Evidence gathering Primarily analytical procedures and enquiries Substantive testing, detailed walkthroughs, independent data verification
Internal controls Controls reviewed at a high level Controls evaluated for design and operating effectiveness
Documentation required Moderate — process descriptions, methodology explanations Extensive — full audit trail, reconciliations, evidence files
Preparation burden Significant Substantial — equivalent to financial statement audit

A common misunderstanding is that limited assurance requires little preparation. In practice, even a limited assurance engagement places considerable demands on an organisation's documentation, governance structures, and data quality. Auditors conducting limited assurance must still obtain sufficient appropriate evidence to support their conclusion.

2.2 How Sustainability Assurance Differs from Financial Auditing

Sustainability assurance shares the same independence requirements, professional scepticism standards, and evidence-gathering disciplines as financial auditing — but it operates across different subject matter with its own specific characteristics.

Key differences that auditors and preparers both need to understand include:

Qualitative as well as quantitative information

Unlike financial statements, sustainability reports include narrative disclosures, policy statements, and forward-looking information. Auditors evaluate whether these qualitative disclosures are accurate, complete, and appropriately presented — not just whether the numbers add up.

Multiple applicable criteria frameworks

Financial statements are prepared under IFRS or GAAP. Sustainability reports may reference ESRS, GRI, ISSB standards, TCFD, or sector-specific frameworks — sometimes several simultaneously. Auditors must understand the criteria being applied before evaluating compliance.

Inherent estimation uncertainty

Many sustainability metrics involve estimation — particularly emissions calculations, water intensity figures, and supply chain data. Auditors evaluate the reasonableness of estimation methods and assumptions, not just arithmetic accuracy.

Broader stakeholder relevance

Financial statements are primarily addressed to investors. Sustainability reports are addressed to a wider range of stakeholders including regulators, employees, civil society, and communities. Auditors consider materiality from this broader perspective.

3. The Regulatory Landscape

The regulatory environment for sustainability assurance has evolved significantly since 2022 and continues to develop across jurisdictions. Understanding the landscape helps organisations identify which requirements apply to them and the timelines they are working towards.

3.1 European Union — CSRD and ESRS

The CSRD represents the most comprehensive mandatory sustainability reporting and assurance regime currently in force. It requires companies within its scope to:

Report against the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)

ESRS cover environmental topics (including ESRS E1 on climate change), social topics, and governance. The double materiality assessment — evaluating both impact materiality and financial materiality — is a defining feature of the CSRD framework.

Obtain independent limited assurance

Initially limited assurance, with the European Commission mandated to develop criteria for reasonable assurance in due course. The assurance engagement must be conducted by an accredited statutory auditor or independent assurance service provider.

Include sustainability reporting in the management report

Unlike standalone sustainability reports, CSRD disclosures are filed in the annual management report — bringing sustainability disclosures into the same legal filing as financial statements and subjecting them to equivalent regulatory scrutiny.

The CSRD's phased implementation began with large listed companies (over 500 employees) reporting from financial year 2024. Large companies not previously required to report under NFRD follow from financial year 2025. Listed SMEs and non-EU companies with significant EU operations follow in subsequent phases.

3.2 United Kingdom

The UK's approach has developed through a combination of mandatory TCFD-aligned disclosures (in force for large listed companies and large LLPs since 2022), the FCA's sustainability disclosure requirements for asset managers, and ongoing development of UK Sustainability Reporting Standards aligned to ISSB.

Mandatory assurance requirements for UK companies are in development. The Financial Reporting Council has published guidance and is monitoring the IAASB's ISSA 5000 framework with a view to UK adoption. Organisations operating across the EU and UK typically align their assurance readiness to the more demanding EU requirements.

3.3 Asia-Pacific

Jurisdictions across APAC are at varying stages of mandatory sustainability reporting and assurance adoption:

Singapore

The Singapore Exchange requires listed companies to report on climate-related disclosures. MAS has proposed mandatory climate-related disclosures for large financial institutions. Assurance requirements are being developed in line with ISSB standards adoption.

Australia

Treasury has finalised mandatory climate-related financial disclosure requirements aligned to ISSB standards, applying to large entities from 2025 with a phased rollout. Assurance requirements are included in the framework, with a transition period for the early phases.

Japan

The Tokyo Stock Exchange's prime market requires TCFD-aligned disclosure for listed companies. Japan is developing sustainability disclosure standards aligned to ISSB, with assurance requirements expected to follow the reporting obligations.

4. What Auditors Evaluate

Understanding what auditors examine during a sustainability assurance engagement is the foundation of effective preparation. Auditors typically evaluate four interconnected areas:

4.1 Data Quality and Integrity

Data quality is the most fundamental area of evaluation. Auditors typically assess:

Data completeness

Whether all relevant data has been captured within the defined reporting boundary. Auditors examine how the organisation identifies its reporting scope — including subsidiaries, joint ventures, and leased assets — and whether data collection covers all entities within that boundary.

Data lineage and traceability

Whether a published figure can be traced back through every transformation to its original source. Auditors look for documented data flows: where data originates, how it is collected, how it is aggregated and calculated, and how it reaches the published disclosure.

Calculation accuracy and methodology consistency

Whether calculations are performed correctly, emission factors are from recognised sources and applied consistently, and methodologies are applied uniformly across the reporting boundary. Prior-period comparability is also examined.

Estimation and assumption transparency

Where estimates are used — for example, extrapolating from meter readings, applying spend-based emission factors, or estimating employee travel data — auditors evaluate whether the estimation approach is reasonable and consistently applied.

4.2 Governance and Oversight

Auditors evaluate the governance structures that oversee sustainability reporting. This includes board-level oversight of sustainability disclosures, the existence of policies and procedures governing data collection and reporting, and the accountability framework for sustainability data quality. Organisations with clearly defined governance — including named accountable individuals at both operational and board level — typically demonstrate a stronger control environment.

4.3 Internal Controls

Internal controls over sustainability reporting are examined in a manner analogous to internal controls over financial reporting. Auditors look for:

Preventive Controls

Stopping errors before they occur

Input validation in data collection systems, access controls limiting who can enter or modify sustainability data, segregation of duties ensuring the person who collects data is not the same person who approves it for disclosure, and documented approval workflows.

Detective Controls

Identifying errors after they occur

Reconciliations between sustainability data and corroborating sources (such as energy consumption reconciled to utility invoices and financial payments), trend analyses comparing current-period data against prior periods, and management review procedures.

Corrective Controls

Addressing errors once identified

Documented error investigation procedures, clear criteria for determining when a correction is material enough to require restatement or disclosure, and evidence that identified errors have been investigated and resolved before data is published.

4.4 Disclosure Completeness and Accuracy

Auditors evaluate whether the sustainability disclosures in the report are complete — covering all material topics identified through the materiality assessment — and whether they accurately reflect the underlying data and the organisation's actual performance. This includes evaluating whether narrative disclosures fairly represent the organisation's situation, without omission of unfavourable information that would render the disclosure misleading.

5. Common Readiness Gaps

Across sustainability assurance engagements, auditors encounter a consistent pattern of preparation gaps. Understanding these common patterns helps organisations identify areas that typically require attention before an engagement commences.

Poor data trails and documentation gaps

Organisations frequently have sustainability data but lack the documentation to demonstrate how it was collected, calculated, and verified. A figure in the published report cannot be traced back through a documented path to its source. Auditors are unable to test data quality without evidence of the data lifecycle.

Inconsistent methodologies

Emission factors sourced from multiple databases without documented rationale, calculation methodologies that vary across business units or reporting periods, and undocumented changes to methodology between years — all undermine the consistency and comparability that auditors require.

Missing governance documentation

Many organisations have informal governance structures — sustainability professionals who understand the reporting process through personal knowledge rather than documented procedures. When those individuals are unavailable, or when the auditor asks to see evidence of oversight, documented governance is absent.

Weak internal controls

Sustainability data is frequently prepared by a single team member, reviewed informally, and published without a documented approval process. The financial reporting principle that preparation, review, and approval should be performed by different individuals is rarely applied to sustainability data.

Incomplete boundary documentation

Organisations often lack clear documentation of their reporting boundary — which entities, facilities, and activities are included, which are excluded, and why. Auditors need to understand and test the boundary before they can evaluate whether the data within it is complete.

Inadequate supplier and value chain data

For Scope 3 emissions and value chain social metrics, many organisations rely on spend-based estimates or extrapolations rather than primary data from suppliers. While this is often legitimate, auditors evaluate whether the approach is clearly documented, consistently applied, and the limitations are disclosed.

6. Building Readiness

Organisations that are well-prepared for sustainability assurance typically share certain structural characteristics. These are not extraordinary capabilities — they are governance, documentation, and process disciplines that parallel those already applied to financial reporting.

6.1 Governance Structures

A clear governance framework for sustainability reporting provides auditors with the foundation they need to understand accountability and oversight. Elements that well-prepared organisations typically have in place include:

Named accountability at board and management level

Clear identification of who at board and senior management level is accountable for sustainability disclosure quality. Under CSRD, the audit committee's oversight mandate explicitly extends to sustainability reporting.

Documented reporting policies and procedures

Written policies governing how sustainability data is collected, calculated, reviewed, and approved. These do not need to be lengthy documents — they need to be clear, current, and known to the people responsible for following them.

Defined roles and responsibilities

A responsibility matrix identifying who owns each sustainability data stream, who reviews it, and who approves it for disclosure. Segregation of duties — separating the preparation, review, and approval roles — is a key characteristic of a well-governed reporting process.

6.2 Data Management

Data management for sustainability reporting typically requires attention to collection, storage, version control, and access management. Organisations that perform well in assurance engagements generally maintain:

Centralised data collection with documented sources

A single system of record for sustainability data, or a clearly documented consolidation process where multiple source systems are used. Every data input has an identified source — a meter reading, a utility invoice, a supplier submission — documented and retained.

Documented calculation methodologies

A methodology register documenting which emission factors, conversion factors, and calculation approaches are applied to each metric. Version-controlled so that changes are tracked and changes in methodology between years can be explained and quantified.

Access controls and audit trails

Data systems with appropriate access controls ensuring only authorised personnel can input or modify sustainability data. Audit trails recording who made changes to data, when, and the nature of the change.

6.3 Documentation and Internal Review

The principle that documentation should be maintained as if it needs to be independently reviewed — because it eventually will be — is the most consistent characteristic of assurance-ready organisations. This involves:

Contemporaneous record-keeping

Evidence created at the time of the process — sign-off records, review notes, approval emails — rather than reconstructed after the fact. Auditors distinguish between evidence of a control operating and evidence constructed to demonstrate that a control existed.

Reconciliation documentation

Documented reconciliations between sustainability data and corroborating sources. Energy consumption reconciled to utility invoices. Fleet fuel consumption reconciled to fuel purchase records. Water withdrawal reconciled to water authority invoices.

Exception and error handling records

A register of data quality issues identified during the reporting period, how each was investigated, what correction was made, and whether the correction was material enough to require disclosure. Auditors view the existence of a functioning exception process positively — it demonstrates that the organisation's controls are actively operating.

7. The Role of Technology

Technology platforms that support sustainability data management can contribute meaningfully to assurance readiness. The characteristics that auditors find most valuable in technology-enabled sustainability reporting are not those that produce attractive dashboards — they are the ones that support the documentation, traceability, and control requirements that assurance demands.

The question auditors ask of a technology platform is not "does it look good?" but "can it demonstrate to us what happened to this data, and who authorised it?"

7.1 Data Lineage and Audit Trails

A platform that maintains an unbroken audit trail from data entry to published disclosure — recording every transformation, calculation, review, and approval — provides a significant advantage in assurance engagements. Auditors can follow the data lifecycle without requiring manual reconstruction by staff, reducing the burden on the organisation and increasing audit efficiency.

Platforms with built-in data lineage documentation allow auditors to select a published figure and trace its provenance: the source data, the individuals who entered it, the calculation applied, the reviewer who checked it, and the approver who released it for consolidation. This is the digital equivalent of a documented controls process.

7.2 Automated Controls

Automated controls embedded in a platform — validation rules that flag anomalous data entries, workflow approvals that enforce segregation of duties, automatic reconciliations that identify discrepancies between data streams — reduce the documentation burden while simultaneously strengthening the control environment.

Organisations using platforms with these capabilities typically find that the evidence-gathering phase of an assurance engagement is substantially more efficient than those relying on manual processes and spreadsheet-based controls.

7.3 Reporting Standards Alignment

Platforms designed with CSRD, ESRS, GRI, and ISSB reporting in mind can facilitate the mapping of data to disclosure requirements — helping organisations identify gaps in their data collection before the assurance engagement identifies them. Auditors evaluate whether the disclosures are complete against the applicable criteria; organisations with systems that support this mapping process are better placed to demonstrate completeness.

8. Timeline and Preparation: What to Expect

Organisations approaching their first sustainability assurance engagement typically benefit from understanding the phases of a typical engagement and the preparation that each phase requires.

1
Pre-Engagement Planning (3–6 months before engagement)
  • Auditors typically conduct a preliminary assessment of the organisation's reporting processes, governance structures, and data management capabilities
  • The scope of the engagement is agreed — which disclosures are subject to assurance, under which standard, and at which assurance level
  • Materiality is discussed: what topics are material, how materiality was determined, and how materiality thresholds affect the scope of evidence-gathering
  • Organisations at this stage typically conduct a self-assessment against the auditor's known requirements — identifying documentation gaps and initiating remediation before fieldwork begins
2
Risk Assessment and Planning
  • Auditors conduct a structured risk assessment, identifying the sustainability topics and data streams that carry the highest risk of material misstatement
  • For each high-risk area, auditors develop specific procedures — the nature, timing, and extent of evidence-gathering
  • Organisations are typically requested to provide process documentation, methodology descriptions, and a controls inventory before fieldwork commences
  • Practical questions are clarified at this stage: the format of working papers, systems access for data testing, and the availability of key personnel during fieldwork
3
Fieldwork and Evidence Gathering
  • Auditors perform their planned procedures: enquiries with responsible personnel, review of documentation, analytical procedures comparing reported data against expectations, and (for reasonable assurance) substantive testing of data against source evidence
  • Control testing involves examining evidence that controls operated as described — sign-off records, reconciliation outputs, system access logs
  • Where gaps or inconsistencies are identified, auditors raise queries with the organisation and evaluate responses and additional evidence provided
  • The audit team documents their findings and the evidence obtained, forming the basis for their conclusions
4
Conclusion and Reporting
  • Auditors communicate findings to management, typically through a management letter identifying significant matters and, where applicable, control deficiencies
  • Material misstatements identified during fieldwork require either correction by the organisation or explanation for why no correction is made
  • The assurance report is issued — for limited assurance, expressing a negative-form conclusion; for reasonable assurance, a positive-form opinion
  • Findings from the engagement typically inform the organisation's preparation for subsequent years, as assurance readiness is an iterative process

9. Conclusion

Sustainability assurance is a technical discipline that rewards preparation. Organisations that understand what auditors evaluate — and build their governance, data management, and documentation practices with that understanding in mind — are better positioned to proceed through assurance engagements efficiently, with fewer findings and greater confidence in their disclosures.

The readiness journey is not short, and it is not simple. But the organisations that approach it systematically — starting with governance, building data quality controls, and documenting their processes — consistently demonstrate that it is achievable within a structured programme of work.

The strongest position in a sustainability assurance engagement is not one of hoping the auditor does not look too closely — it is one of being able to demonstrate, at every stage, that the data has been through a rigorous process.

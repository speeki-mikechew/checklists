Organisational Governance

What Auditors Look For: Organisational Governance

Auditors assess whether social responsibility is genuinely embedded in governance structures or exists only as a superficial commitment. They examine board-level accountability, decision-making transparency, and evidence that social responsibility considerations influence strategic choices.

Key evidence includes board meeting minutes discussing SR topics, documented governance policies referencing ISO 26000 principles, and clear accountability assignments for social responsibility outcomes.

Social responsibility assigned to junior staff without board oversight or strategic authority signals weak governance integration.
Strong governance is the foundation of ISO 26000 - without board-level commitment, other core subjects cannot be effectively addressed.
2

Human Rights

What Auditors Look For: Human Rights

Auditors evaluate the rigour of human rights due diligence processes, looking for systematic identification of risks across operations and the value chain. They expect documented assessments that go beyond tier-one suppliers to identify salient human rights issues.

Evidence of functioning grievance mechanisms accessible to affected stakeholders is essential, along with records demonstrating how identified impacts were remediated.

Lack of documented remedy actions where human rights impacts have been identified indicates due diligence without meaningful follow-through.
Human rights due diligence is increasingly mandated by legislation globally - gaps here create both ethical and legal exposure.
3

Labour Practices

What Auditors Look For: Labour Practices

Auditors examine whether labour practices extend equitably to all workers, including contractors, temporary staff, and those in the supply chain. They look for documented health and safety management systems with incident tracking and root cause analysis.

Evidence of social dialogue mechanisms, training records, and fair remuneration policies (including gender pay gap analysis) demonstrates commitment to worker welfare.

Contractor workforce excluded from health and safety metrics or training programmes indicates inconsistent application of labour standards.
Labour practices directly affect employee wellbeing, productivity, and organisational reputation - they are often the first area stakeholders scrutinise.
4

The Environment

What Auditors Look For: The Environment

Auditors assess environmental performance through quantified metrics on emissions, resource consumption, waste generation, and biodiversity impacts. They expect evidence of life-cycle thinking in product design and documented climate strategies with measurable targets.

Environmental management systems (ISO 14001 alignment), pollution prevention programmes, and circular economy initiatives demonstrate mature environmental practices.

Absence of Scope 3 emissions tracking or climate targets indicates incomplete environmental accountability across the value chain.
Environmental stewardship is increasingly linked to financial performance through regulatory requirements, resource costs, and stakeholder expectations.
5

Fair Operating Practices

What Auditors Look For: Fair Operating Practices

Auditors evaluate anti-corruption programmes for comprehensiveness, including policies, risk assessments, training records, and third-party due diligence procedures. They examine competitive practices and political engagement for ethical boundaries.

Supply chain responsibility is critical - auditors look for documented supplier codes of conduct, assessment processes, and evidence that social responsibility requirements flow through procurement decisions.

Anti-corruption training limited to senior management or gifts and hospitality registers not systematically reviewed suggests superficial compliance.
Fair operating practices protect organisational reputation and prevent legal exposure from corruption, anti-competitive behaviour, or supply chain misconduct.
6

Consumer Issues

What Auditors Look For: Consumer Issues

Auditors examine marketing materials and product information for accuracy and transparency. They assess consumer health and safety through product testing records, recall procedures, and incident response protocols.

Data protection practices, complaint resolution systems, and accessibility considerations for vulnerable consumers are key areas of focus for demonstrating consumer-centric responsibility.

Consumer complaint data not systematically analysed for root causes or product safety recalls handled reactively rather than proactively.
Consumer trust is earned through transparent practices and responsive service - failures here directly impact brand reputation and market position.
7

Community Involvement and Development

What Auditors Look For: Community Involvement and Development

Auditors assess whether community engagement is genuine and responsive to local priorities rather than corporate-driven philanthropy. They look for evidence of community consultation, needs assessments, and programmes designed in partnership with local stakeholders.

Documentation of local hiring practices, skills development initiatives, local procurement policies, and health promotion activities demonstrates substantive community commitment.

Community investment programmes designed without community input or impact measurement indicates tokenistic rather than meaningful engagement.
Organisations operate within communities - genuine investment in community development builds social licence and long-term business sustainability.
8

Stakeholder Identification and Engagement

What Auditors Look For: Stakeholder Identification and Engagement

Auditors evaluate stakeholder mapping processes for comprehensiveness and whether engagement approaches are tailored to different stakeholder groups. They expect documented stakeholder engagement plans with clear objectives, methods, and feedback loops.

Evidence that stakeholder input genuinely influences organisational decisions - not just consultation for appearance - is essential. Transparency in communicating both positive performance and challenges builds credibility.

Stakeholder engagement limited to shareholders and customers while excluding workers, communities, or civil society groups signals incomplete stakeholder analysis.
Effective stakeholder engagement is the mechanism through which organisations understand expectations, identify risks, and build the relationships needed for sustainable success.

Red Flags in Social Responsibility

Lack of Governance Oversight

Social responsibility relegated to junior staff without board-level accountability or strategic integration.

Human Rights Blind Spots

No systematic assessment of human rights risks in operations or supply chain, particularly in high-risk regions.

Poor Labour Conditions

Evidence of unfair treatment, unsafe working conditions, or suppression of workers' rights to organise.

Environmental Degradation

Operations causing significant pollution, resource depletion, or biodiversity loss without mitigation plans.

Corruption Vulnerabilities

Weak anti-corruption controls, opaque political involvement, or unfair competitive practices.

Stakeholder Exclusion

Limited or tokenistic engagement with affected communities, workers, or other key stakeholders.

Self-Assessment Questions

How does your organisation balance financial performance with social and environmental considerations?

Assess whether social responsibility is truly integrated into strategy or treated as a secondary concern.

Which stakeholder groups have the most influence on your decisions, and are any groups systematically excluded?

Identify potential blind spots in stakeholder engagement that could lead to adverse impacts.

What mechanisms exist for workers, communities, or consumers to raise concerns without fear of retaliation?

Effective grievance mechanisms are essential for identifying and addressing social responsibility failures.

How do you verify that social responsibility commitments extend throughout your supply chain?

Many social responsibility risks exist in the value chain beyond direct operations.

What measures track progress on social responsibility, and are these reported transparently to stakeholders?

Meaningful accountability requires clear metrics, regular monitoring, and public disclosure of performance.

What Auditors Look For: ISO 26000 Social Responsibility Red Flags

When assessing social responsibility management systems, auditors commonly flag the following governance, human rights, and labour practice concerns.

Governance Red Flags
  • Board composition does not include ESG expertise
  • Stakeholder engagement conducted ad hoc, not systematically
  • No documented due diligence process for social impacts
Human Rights Red Flags
  • Supply chain due diligence limited to tier 1 suppliers
  • Grievance mechanisms not accessible to affected communities
  • No evidence of remedy where impacts identified
Labour Practice Red Flags
  • Contractor workforce excluded from health and safety metrics
  • No gender pay gap analysis or disclosure
  • Training records incomplete for safety-critical roles

ISO 37002 Whistleblowing Management Systems Checklist

12 areas auditors evaluate when assessing whistleblowing management system effectiveness and ISO 37002 compliance. This checklist reflects the areas independent auditors evaluate when providing assurance over your whistleblowing system.

1

Assess the current state of your whistleblowing management system

What Auditors Look For

Auditors evaluate whether organisations have conducted a genuine baseline assessment of their whistleblowing system. They look for documented evidence of current reporting volumes, trend analysis over time, and honest evaluation of system gaps.

Red flag: No historical data on report volumes or types, or inability to explain why report numbers are unusually low compared to industry benchmarks.
Why this matters: A thorough current-state assessment demonstrates management commitment and provides the foundation for measuring improvement. Auditors view this as essential evidence that the system is genuinely managed, not just implemented.
2

Define the context and scope of your whistleblowing management system

What Auditors Look For

Auditors assess whether the whistleblowing system scope genuinely covers all relevant stakeholders, locations, and languages. They verify that accessibility has been tested across different user groups, including remote workers, contractors, and non-native speakers.

Red flag: Reporting channels only available in one language despite multinational operations, or no consideration of how factory workers or field staff can submit reports.
Why this matters: A system that excludes portions of the workforce cannot be effective. Auditors view scope gaps as fundamental design flaws that undermine the entire whistleblowing programme.
3

Get leadership commitment

What Auditors Look For

Auditors seek evidence of visible leadership commitment beyond policy signatures. They look for board minutes discussing whistleblowing, executive communications promoting speak-up culture, and documented instances where leadership acted on reports without retaliation.

Red flag: Leadership approval exists only on paper, with no evidence of active promotion or discussion at board level. Any documented retaliation incidents that went unaddressed.
Why this matters: Without genuine leadership commitment, whistleblowing systems fail to generate trust. Auditors recognize that tone-at-the-top directly influences whether employees feel safe reporting concerns.
4

Set up a whistleblowing policy

What Auditors Look For

Auditors examine whether the whistleblowing policy is comprehensive, clearly written, and accessible. They verify that it defines reportable concerns, explains the reporting process, and explicitly commits to non-retaliation with specific consequences for violations.

Red flag: Policy uses vague language, lacks clear definitions of wrongdoing, or buries reporting instructions in lengthy legal text. No explicit anti-retaliation commitment with enforcement mechanisms.
Why this matters: The policy is the foundation of the entire whistleblowing programme. If employees cannot easily understand how to report or what protections they have, the system cannot function effectively.
5

Define roles, responsibilities and authority

What Auditors Look For

Auditors verify that specific individuals are assigned responsibility for the whistleblowing function with appropriate independence, authority, and resources. They assess whether investigators have relevant qualifications and sufficient training on handling sensitive matters.

Red flag: Whistleblowing function assigned as additional duty with no dedicated time allocation, or investigators have conflicts of interest with business units they investigate.
Why this matters: Clear accountability ensures reports are handled properly. Auditors view inadequate resourcing or conflicted roles as indicators that the organisation is not treating whistleblowing seriously.
6

Create a plan for the management system

What Auditors Look For

Auditors review whether the organisation has documented objectives for the whistleblowing system with measurable targets. They assess implementation plans, timeline adherence, and evidence that resources were allocated as planned.

Red flag: No documented implementation plan, objectives stated only in general terms without metrics, or significant gaps between planned and actual rollout.
Why this matters: A clear plan demonstrates intentional system design rather than ad-hoc implementation. Auditors use planning documentation to assess management commitment and capability.
7

Generate awareness and provide training

What Auditors Look For

Auditors examine training records to verify all employees received whistleblowing awareness training, with enhanced training for managers and investigators. They assess whether training covers practical scenarios and is refreshed periodically.

Red flag: Training completion rates below 90%, no evidence of manager-specific training on handling disclosures, or training content not updated for three or more years.
Why this matters: Awareness is prerequisite to reporting. Auditors recognize that even well-designed systems fail if employees do not know how to use them or managers mishandle initial disclosures.
8

Create a documentation plan

What Auditors Look For

Auditors assess whether case documentation is complete, secure, and retained according to policy. They verify access controls exist, audit trails are maintained, and retention periods comply with legal requirements across all operating jurisdictions.

Red flag: Investigation files stored in shared drives without access restrictions, no documented retention policy, or evidence of premature deletion of case records.
Why this matters: Proper documentation provides evidence of due process and enables trend analysis. Auditors view documentation failures as both a compliance risk and indicator of system immaturity.
9

Set rules around confidentiality

What Auditors Look For

Auditors verify that confidentiality controls are technically implemented, not just promised. They examine whether systems prevent identity disclosure, how need-to-know access is determined, and whether anonymity options genuinely protect reporter identity.

Red flag: System logs IP addresses or metadata that could identify anonymous reporters, confidentiality relies solely on policy rather than technical controls, or broad access to case details.
Why this matters: Confidentiality breaches destroy trust and can expose reporters to retaliation. Auditors consider confidentiality protection as fundamental to whistleblowing system integrity.

What Auditors Look For: ISO 37002 Red Flags

When providing assurance over whistleblowing management systems, auditors commonly flag the following areas as concerns. Understanding these red flags helps ensure your system meets verification standards.

System Design Red Flags
  • Reporting channels not accessible anonymously
  • Investigation function reports to management being investigated
  • No defined escalation matrix for sensitive reports
  • Case management system without access controls or audit trail
Operational Red Flags
  • Reports closed without documented investigation rationale
  • Average investigation time exceeding 90 days without explanation
  • Lack of reporter feedback loop (even if anonymous)
  • No evidence of root cause analysis for substantiated cases
Governance Red Flags
  • Board receives only summary statistics, not case themes
  • No review of cases involving senior management by independent parties
  • Retaliation allegations not tracked or reported separately
  • No periodic testing of channel effectiveness (e.g., dummy reports)
